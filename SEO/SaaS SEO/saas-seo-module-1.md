# SaaS SEO Course
## Module 1 — Understanding the SaaS Product Before Touching a Keyword

**Prerequisite:** Semantic SEO Course + AEO Course
**Who this module is for:** Agency SEOs already running client campaigns who need SaaS-specific depth
**Module outcome:** Produce a completed Product Intelligence Document before any keyword research begins

---

## Why This Module Exists

Most SaaS SEO programmes fail at the research stage, not the execution stage.

The SEO team opens Ahrefs, types the product name, finds competitors, and starts copying their topical maps. Six months later, traffic is up, signups are flat, and nobody can explain why.

The answer is usually found before the first keyword was ever searched: the team never understood what the product actually does, who it does it for, and what those people search before they know software is the solution.

This module fixes that. It runs before keyword research. Without it, everything that follows is built on the wrong foundation.

---

## Lesson 1.1 — The Three Questions That Define Your Entire Strategy

Most SaaS clients hand you a brief that says something like: "We make project management software for remote teams. Target competitors X and Y."

That brief tells you nothing useful.

Three questions produce the inputs keyword research actually needs. Get answers to all three before opening any tool.

---

**Question 1: What problem does this software solve?**

Not "what does it do." What problem does the buyer have before they search?

The answer rarely comes from the product team. They describe features. The answer comes from customers who switched to the product recently.

Bob Moesta's switch interview technique surfaces the trigger event. Ask customers: "Take me back to the day you first thought you needed something different. What was happening?" Their answer in their own words is your problem statement.

Twine, a SaaS intranet tool, ran this exercise. For eight years the company described itself as an "intranet platform." Customer interviews revealed that almost no buyer used the word "intranet." They described "connecting teams," "centralising docs," and "stopping people from losing things in Slack." The company rewrote its entire content strategy around those verbs, not the category label.

**The output:** A one-sentence problem statement in customer language. Not product language.

---

**Question 2: Who has that problem?**

For B2C SaaS: one person with one job to do. For B2B SaaS: a buying committee. Gartner's 2024 data puts the average at 6.8 stakeholders per B2B SaaS deal, up from 5.4 in 2020. Enterprise deals average 13.

Each stakeholder has a different problem. The Head of People who buys an intranet tool is trying to "build culture across distributed offices." The CFO signing the same deal is trying to "reduce risk from information scattered across personal drives." Same product, different jobs, different content needs.

At B2C or product-led-growth (PLG) scale, the person and the decision-maker are the same. The sales cycle is hours to days, not months. That difference rewrites your entire content architecture, covered in Lesson 1.6.

**The output:** One job-to-be-done statement per buyer role in the format: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

---

**Question 3: What do they search before they know your solution exists?**

This is the question most SEO teams skip entirely.

They target category keywords: "project management software," "team collaboration tool," "intranet platform." These are searched by people who already know a software solution exists. They are in evaluation mode, not discovery mode.

The problem-aware searcher types "how to stop losing files in Slack" or "how to get remote team to use the same docs." They have no idea an intranet tool solves this. An article that targets those queries, solves the problem, and introduces the product naturally is a traffic and signup machine.

Grow and Convert documented the conversion gap across 64 articles and 200,000+ organic visitors on a SaaS client. Bottom-of-funnel content converted at 4.78%. Top-of-funnel "awareness" content that was not JTBD-aligned converted at 0.19%. That is a 25x difference. The gap is not the funnel stage. It is the precision of the problem match.

**The output:** A list of pre-category queries that describe the problem your product solves, in the language customers use before they know software exists.

---

**Common Mistake: Building from competitor keywords**

Copying a competitor's topical map anchors your strategy to their positioning. You end up ranking for their audience, not yours. Their best-fit customer and your best-fit customer are often not the same person. Start from the problem, not from what someone else is already ranking for.

---

**Summary**

- Question 1 (the problem) comes from customer interviews, not the product team.
- Question 2 (the audience) requires a job-to-be-done layer, not just a demographic profile.
- Question 3 (the queries) requires finding problem-language searches before category-language searches.

*Next: Once you have these answers, you have the inputs to understand what the product actually does in search terms. That is product-led content, covered next.*

## Lesson 1.2 — Product-Led Content

Most SaaS content either ignores the product entirely or reads like a product brochure. Neither converts.

**Product-led content** is content that uses the product to solve the reader's problem. The product appears because it is genuinely the best tool for the job being illustrated, not because the brief said "mention the product."

---

**The Ahrefs Business Potential Score**

Ahrefs built an internal filter before committing to any content topic. They call it the Business Potential Score. It runs from 0 to 3.

| Score | What It Means | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Product is the obvious, natural solution to this query | Write it. High priority. |
| 2 | Product fits naturally as one of several solutions | Write it. Acceptable. |
| 1 | Product mention feels forced or tangential | Deprioritise. |
| 0 | Product cannot be mentioned naturally | Do not write. |

Ahrefs CMO Tim Soulo's framing: "People first learn how to use your tools, and they sign up because they know how to use your tool." The content teaches the job. The product demonstrates it.

The filter is enforceable. When a client pushes for a high-volume keyword that scores 0 on Business Potential, you have a documented framework to push back.

---

**What product-led content looks like in practice**

Ahrefs writes "25 marketing metrics worth tracking." At six natural points, Ahrefs features appear with annotated screenshots showing how to pull those metrics. The content teaches the concept. The product demonstrates how to action it.

Zapier writes "best to-do list apps." Every app on the list integrates with Zapier. Each entry shows the specific Zap available. The list is genuinely useful. The product tie-in is structural, not forced.

WP Rocket writes "how to fix a slow WordPress site." Every fix either directly uses WP Rocket or explains why WP Rocket handles it. The article ranks for "wordpress slow" queries. Every reader is a potential customer.

---

**What product-led content is not**

| Not product-led content | Why |
|---|---|
| Feature announcement posts | Audience is existing customers, not prospects |
| Help docs and knowledge base | Post-signup content, not acquisition content |
| Thought leadership with no product connection | No signup path for the reader |
| High-volume TOFU post with Business Potential Score of 0 | Traffic with no conversion mechanism |
| Article that forces a product mention into an unrelated topic | Reads like an ad, not content |

The distinction matters operationally. When you brief content for a SaaS client, every topic needs a Business Potential Score before the brief is approved. A topic that scores 0 is not a SaaS SEO asset. It is a general content piece with a SaaS logo on it.

---

**Common Mistake: Chasing high-volume topics that cannot mention the product**

The temptation is real. "Remote work tips" has 40,000 monthly searches. A project management SaaS can write it. The Business Potential Score is 0 or 1. The conversion rate is sub-0.5%.

The same team writes "how to run a sprint retrospective remotely." Search volume is 800/month. Business Potential Score is 3. The product is the natural solution. Conversion rate is 3-5%.

Traffic from the first article does not justify the content investment. Start from the Business Potential Score, not the volume.

---

**Summary**

- Product-led content uses the product to solve the reader's problem. The product appears because it fits, not because it was mandated.
- Score every topic 0-3 for Business Potential before briefing it. Reject everything below 2 unless there is a deliberate strategic reason.
- The Grow and Convert 25x conversion gap is the business case for this filter. Use it with clients.

*Next: Scoring individual topics requires knowing what every feature of the product actually solves. That is the Problem-Solution-Feature matrix.*

## Lesson 1.3 — The Problem-Solution-Feature Matrix

A SaaS product ships features. Features do not rank. Problems rank.

The Problem-Solution-Feature (PSF) matrix is the translation layer between what the product does and what your audience searches for. It is the document that makes keyword research produce relevant results instead of category noise.

---

**The matrix structure**

| Problem (customer language) | Solution (job-to-be-done) | Feature (product capability) | Target query | BPS |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "My WordPress site is slow on mobile" | Reduce LCP without writing code | WP Rocket lazy-load + cache | "speed up wordpress mobile" | 3 |
| "Passport photo background is wrong colour" | Replace background to white/blue spec | Remove.bg background swap | "passport photo white background" | 3 |
| "Slack messages not tracked as tasks" | Convert messages into trackable tasks | Zapier Slack-to-Asana integration | "slack asana integration" | 3 |
| "I don't know which channels drive revenue" | Multi-touch attribution dashboard | HubSpot attribution reporting | "marketing attribution tracking" | 2 |

Build this matrix before researching a single keyword. The Problem column is the column that generates search queries.

---

**How to build the matrix**

1. List every feature the product ships. Use the pricing-tier table or changelog, not the marketing copy.
2. For each feature, find three to five real customer complaints that the feature resolves. Source: G2 reviews, support tickets, sales call recordings, Reddit threads.
3. Write the problem in customer language. Not "insufficient performance optimisation." "My site takes six seconds to load on mobile."
4. Write the JTBD statement. "When my site is slow on mobile, I want to fix it without touching code, so I can stop losing visitors before the page loads."
5. Find the search queries that match the problem statement using Autocomplete, PAA, and Reddit.
6. Score each query cluster 0-3 for Business Potential.
7. Remove any feature where no problem maps to a real query. That feature either has no search demand or is positioned incorrectly.

---

**The Remove.bg example**

Remove.bg has one feature: background removal. The PSF matrix produces 50+ pages, each targeting a distinct JTBD.

The /f/ URL pattern shows the architecture: /f/passport-photo-background-change, /f/blue-background-passport-photo, /f/transparent-background, /f/white-background, /f/remove-logo-background. Plus vertical hubs: Photographers, Ecommerce, Car Dealerships, Real Estate, Developers, Media, Enterprise.

Same capability. Fifty-plus entry points. Each one targets a different customer problem.

The matrix forces this thinking. Without it, a team writes one generic "background removal tool" page and stops.

---

**Common Mistake: Building the matrix from the feature list down**

The matrix must be built problem-first, not feature-first. A product team thinks in features. A buyer thinks in problems. Start from the Problem column. If a feature produces no entry in the Problem column, either the feature has no SEO value or the interviews were not thorough enough.

---

**Summary**

- The PSF matrix translates product features into customer problems into search queries.
- Build it before keyword research. The Problem column is the keyword input.
- Remove.bg's 50+ use-case pages come from one feature mapped through a comprehensive PSF matrix.

*Next: The matrix tells you what to target. The audience profile tells you who is searching and how their buying behaviour shapes what you produce.*

## Lesson 1.4 — Audience Profiling for SaaS

You know the Semantic SEO audience profiling process. For SaaS, one layer is missing from every profile that underperforms: the job-to-be-done.

Demographics and psychographics describe who the person is. The JTBD layer describes what they are trying to accomplish and why they are searching right now.

---

**Three-layer SaaS audience profile**

| Layer | What It Captures | How to Get It |
|---|---|---|
| Demographics | Company size, role, industry, geography, tech stack | CRM data, ICP analysis, LinkedIn filters |
| Psychographics | Beliefs, fears, language patterns, current workarounds | Sales recordings, support tickets, community posts |
| Jobs-to-be-done | The functional, emotional, social outcome they hire the product to deliver | Switch interviews using the Moesta script |

---

**The switch interview script**

Run this with five to ten customers who signed up in the last 90 days. They remember the switch clearly.

1. Take me back to the moment you first thought you needed something different from what you were using.
2. What were you using before? What specifically was wrong with it?
3. Walk me through the day you decided to evaluate something new.
4. Who else was involved in the decision?
5. What almost stopped you from switching?
6. What did you expect to be different after you switched?

Questions 1 and 2 give you the trigger event and the problem statement. Question 5 surfaces anxieties that become FAQ content and objection-handling pages. Question 6 gives you the success outcome that becomes your pillar page angle.

---

**B2B SaaS: profile per role, not per company**

For B2B SaaS, build one profile per buying-committee role. The average B2B SaaS deal involves 6.8 stakeholders (Gartner, 2024). Each role hires the product for a different job.

Example: a workforce management SaaS.

| Role | JTBD statement | Content priority |
|---|---|---|
| HR Director | "When headcount doubles, I want a single system for schedules, so I can stop managing rotas in spreadsheets." | "how to manage staff schedules at scale" |
| CFO | "When I review labour costs, I want real-time overage visibility, so I can stop getting surprise payroll variances." | "labour cost reporting software" |
| Shift Manager | "When a shift is uncovered, I want to find cover without calling 15 people, so I can focus on the floor." | "fill empty shift quickly" |
| IT Manager | "When HR adds a new system, I want it to connect to our HRIS, so I can avoid a data migration project." | "[product] [HRIS name] integration" |

Each role produces different keyword clusters, different content formats, and different CTAs. A single "HR software for growing teams" brief serves none of them well.

---

**B2C and PLG SaaS: profile is simpler, intent matters more**

For B2C or PLG SaaS, you have one decision-maker and a conversion window measured in hours. The JTBD frame still applies but the content implication is different.

Short, fast-loading, action-first content with a self-serve conversion path. Not a demo request. A free trial button at the point of problem resolution.

---

**Common Mistake: Treating personas as static demographic files**

A persona without a JTBD and a switch trigger does not tell you what to write. "Marketing Manager, 28-35, North America, uses Slack" tells you nothing about what she searches when she has a problem your product solves.

The JTBD layer transforms the persona from a description into a content brief input.

---

**Summary**

- Three layers: demographics, psychographics, JTBD. All three are required.
- For B2B SaaS, build one profile per buying-committee role, not per company type.
- The switch interview script produces the JTBD layer. Five to ten interviews is enough to find the patterns.

*Next: You know the audience. Now identify exactly where the product sits in the market so you know which category keywords to target and which competitors to build pages against.*

## Lesson 1.5 — Identifying the Product Category

The product category determines your head terms, your comparison page targets, and your alternative page strategy. Get it wrong and you rank for the wrong audience.

Most SaaS teams pick the largest adjacent category, thinking bigger category means more traffic. It does. It also means more competition and lower conversion because the traffic is not specific enough.

---

**April Dunford's five-component framework**

Each component produces a direct SEO output.

| Component | Question | SEO output |
|---|---|---|
| Competitive alternatives | What do buyers use today instead of your product? Include spreadsheets and doing nothing. | Comparison pages and "alternatives to X" pages |
| Unique attributes | What can your product do that alternatives cannot? | Feature pages and use-case pages |
| Value | What outcome does the unique attribute deliver that buyers care about? | Pillar page angle and H1 framing |
| Best-fit customers | Which specific segment gets the most value? | Vertical landing pages and ICP content |
| Market category | What existing context makes the value immediately obvious? | Category head terms and definition pages |

---

**Three positioning plays and their SEO implications**

**Play 1: Position inside an existing category.** Use when buyers already search a category label and your product fits it. DocuSign in "e-signature software."

SEO implications: target the category head term, write a "what is [category]" pillar, build comparison and alternative pages against all named competitors.

**Play 2: Position against a specific competitor as a sub-category.** Use when you serve a segment the category leader underserves.

Userlist repositioned from "email automation for SaaS" to "email marketing platform for B2B SaaS." Their public account: "Calling out our primary competitors by name had a magic effect. Now potential customers instantly grasp what we do." SEO implications: "[Competitor] alternative" pages, "[Your product] vs [Competitor]" pages, and pillar content around the sub-category pain points.

**Play 3: Position around the problem, not the category.** Use when no category term exists or when buyers search the problem before the solution.

Zapier did not rank for "workflow automation software" early. It ranked for "connect [App A] to [App B]" because buyers searched specific integrations, not a category. SEO implications: JTBD problem queries dominate. Build the PSF matrix first.

---

**Three-question decision tree**

1. Can a buyer describe what the product does in five words using an existing category label? Position inside the category.
2. Do best-fit customers compare the product against two to three named competitors? Position against them specifically.
3. Do best-fit customers come from spreadsheets or manual processes? JTBD problem queries beat category queries.

---

**Common Mistake: Choosing the broadest category to maximise traffic**

A niche AI scheduling tool that positions itself as "AI productivity software" enters one of the most competitive SERPs on the internet. Traffic potential is theoretically higher. Conversion rate is near zero because the audience is too broad.

Win a narrow category completely. Then expand.

---

**Summary**

- Five Dunford components each produce a specific SEO deliverable. Map them explicitly before keyword research.
- Three positioning plays require different SEO strategies. Identify which applies before building the keyword architecture.
- Specificity beats breadth. Own a narrow category completely rather than ranking weakly for a broad one.

*Next: B2C and B2B SaaS require different content strategies at every funnel stage. The sales cycle length alone changes the page type, the CTA, and the content depth required.*

## Lesson 1.6 — B2B vs B2C SaaS: Where the Strategy Diverges

You cannot run the same SEO programme on a B2B SaaS with a 90-day sales cycle and a B2C SaaS with a same-session conversion.

---

**The core divergence table**

| Dimension | B2B SaaS | B2C / PLG SaaS |
|---|---|---|
| Median sales cycle | 84 days (Optifai, 2025, N=939) | Hours to 7 days |
| Stakeholders | 6.8 average; 13 at enterprise | 1 |
| Keyword volume expectations | 50-500/month per term is often acceptable | Volume matters |
| Keyword economics | 200 searches, each a potential $20K ARR opportunity | 20,000 searches, each a potential $15/month |
| Content depth | 2,000-5,000 word pillars, ROI calculators, security pages | Short, fast, action-first |
| CTA type | Demo, sales call, trial with sales follow-up | Self-serve sign-up, free trial, install |
| Killer page types | "vs" pages, alternatives, ROI/business case, security, integrations | Free tool, template gallery, programmatic use cases |

---

**The sales cycle drives content architecture**

B2B SaaS sales cycle by contract value (Optifai, 2025, N=939 deals):

| ACV | Typical cycle |
|---|---|
| Under $15K (SMB) | 14 to 30 days |
| $15K to $50K (mid-market) | 30 to 90 days |
| $50K to $100K | 60 to 120 days |
| $100K to $250K (enterprise) | 90 to 180 days |
| Over $250K | 180 to 365 days |

Four rules for sales-cycle-driven content:

1. Under 7-day cycle: one page can convert. Optimise for intent clarity and friction removal.
2. 7 to 30-day cycle: build a sequence. Problem page leads to comparison page leads to trial CTA.
3. 30 to 90-day cycle: add a champion enablement layer. ROI content, internal-pitch templates, case studies the buyer can share internally.
4. 90-day-plus cycle: add a procurement layer. Security pages (SOC 2, GDPR, data residency), pricing transparency, implementation documentation. These pages are read by people in the buying committee who never contact sales.

---

**B2B-specific content with no B2C equivalent**

Security and compliance pages. At mid-market and enterprise, procurement teams run security reviews. A dedicated security page covering SOC 2, GDPR, and SSO availability removes a blocker that adds two to four weeks to the sales cycle. It also ranks for "[product] SOC 2" and "[product] GDPR compliance."

ROI and business case content. Champions in B2B need to sell the purchase internally. A page titled "How to calculate the ROI of [product]" with a real calculator serves the champion's internal job. It ranks for "ROI of [category]" queries.

Integration pages. A dedicated page for every major tool in the buyer's stack (Salesforce, HubSpot, Slack, Jira) ranks for "[product] [tool] integration" queries and removes a technical objection before sales is contacted.

---

**B2C/PLG-specific content with no B2B equivalent**

Free tools as SEO assets. HubSpot's Blog Topic Generator, Canva's template gallery, Remove.bg's free background remover. The tool is the product's core capability in a free, no-signup version. It ranks for the problem query. It converts visitors into users directly. This model does not translate to B2B because the enterprise buyer is not the end user.

Programmatic use-case pages. Canva runs roughly 21,000 /templates/ pages covering every design format a user might need. Remove.bg's /f/ architecture covers every background removal use case. These work for PLG SaaS because the user can self-serve immediately. They do not work for B2B SaaS with a 90-day sales cycle.

---

**Common Mistake: Applying a B2C playbook to a B2B motion**

This is the most common cause of "rankings up, pipeline flat" in SaaS SEO audits. High-volume informational content with a free-trial CTA. Product with an 84-day sales cycle that requires a sales conversation to close. Traffic comes in. Nobody in procurement is clicking a free trial button.

Identify the sales cycle on day one. It determines everything downstream.

---

**Summary**

- B2B and B2C SaaS diverge on sales cycle length, stakeholder count, keyword economics, content depth, and CTA type.
- Sales cycle length is the single most important input to content architecture. Match the content layer to the cycle stage.
- B2B requires security pages, ROI content, and champion enablement content. B2C requires free tools, programmatic pages, and frictionless sign-up flows.

- B2B and B2C SaaS require different content architectures at every funnel stage. The sales cycle length is the decision variable. Identify it on day one.
