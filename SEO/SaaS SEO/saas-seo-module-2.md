# SaaS SEO Course
## Module 2 — The SaaS Keyword Architecture

**Prerequisite:** Module 1 complete. Product Intelligence Document signed off.
**Module outcome:** A complete SaaS Keyword Architecture Spreadsheet — every keyword cluster mapped to a funnel layer, a page type, and a priority stage before a single brief is written.

---

## Why Keyword Architecture Is Different in SaaS

Most keyword research produces a list. SaaS keyword architecture produces a system.

The list tells you which words to use. The system tells you which words belong on which page, in which order, targeting which buyer at which stage of a 14-to-180-day decision process.

Get the architecture wrong and you get the most common failure in SaaS SEO: traffic grows, signups stay flat. The content is attracting the right industry but the wrong intent stage. Someone researching a general topic lands on a product-specific page. Someone ready to sign up lands on an educational guide. The funnel leaks at every stage because the keyword allocation was never mapped to the funnel in the first place.

This module builds the architecture before a single brief is written.

---

## Lesson 2.1 — Why Traditional Keyword Research Fails for SaaS

Traditional keyword research ranks topics by search volume, filters by difficulty, and assigns the highest-volume, lowest-difficulty terms to the first articles. This process optimises for traffic. SaaS SEO needs to optimise for signups.

The gap between the two is measurable. Grow and Convert's clients see bottom-of-funnel content converting at 5-7% compared to 1-2% for typical top-of-funnel articles. That gap exists because high-volume keywords attract early-stage researchers who are nowhere near a purchase decision. Low-volume, high-intent keywords attract buyers who are days away from signing up.

Traditional keyword research misses SaaS in three specific ways.

**First, it ignores the sales cycle.** A B2B SaaS keyword with 200 monthly searches can represent more pipeline than a 20,000-search informational term. If each of those 200 searchers has a problem worth $20K ARR to solve, the economics are completely different from a B2C traffic play. Volume-first research never surfaces this.

**Second, it conflates intent stages.** Sorting by volume produces a mixed list of awareness-stage, evaluation-stage, and purchase-stage keywords with no allocation logic. You end up briefing a mix of content types with no funnel sequencing.

**Third, it misses the "sprinkle method" trap.** Most companies spend most of their SEO effort prioritising high search volume but low buying intent keywords, and try to rank for many different keywords with individual pages — what Grow and Convert calls "The Sprinkle Method" — sprinkling keywords into pages hoping to rank for several terms. Each page targets no intent well. Every page converts weakly.

SaaS keyword architecture fixes all three by assigning every keyword a funnel layer, a conversion role, and a page type before any writing begins.

---

**Common Mistake: Starting with the competitor's keywords**

Copying a competitor's keyword list imports their positioning and their topical gaps simultaneously. Their highest-volume pages are usually their weakest converters. You replicate the strategy without knowing which pages are actually driving their signups.

---

**Summary**

- Volume-first keyword research optimises for traffic. SaaS needs to optimise for signups.
- The conversion gap between high-intent and low-intent SaaS content is 3-5x. The architecture exists to maximise time spent on high-intent keywords first.
- Traditional keyword research has three specific failure modes in SaaS: ignoring sales cycle economics, conflating intent stages, and producing sprinkle-method content with no funnel allocation.

## Lesson 2.2 — The SaaS Funnel Keyword Model

Every keyword in a SaaS programme belongs to one of four layers. Each layer has a different intent, a different page type, a different CTA, and a different conversion rate expectation.

| Layer | Label | User State | Intent | Conversion Rate | Page Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Problem-aware | Has the problem, no solution awareness | Informational | 0.5-2% | Blog, guide, data study |
| 2 | Category-aware | Knows software exists, evaluating options | Commercial investigation | 3-8% | Comparison, alternative, feature, use-case |
| 3 | Product-aware | Evaluating your product specifically | Transactional | 8-20% | Landing page, pricing, demo, trial |
| 4 | Retention | Existing user, wants more value | Informational + navigational | Not applicable | Docs, help, integration guides |

The conversion rates are approximate benchmarks from practitioner data. Comparison pages ranking for "your product vs competitor" with 200 monthly searches and a 15% conversion rate are worth more than a guide ranking for a 5,000-volume informational term at 1% conversion.

The model has two strategic implications.

**First, the layers run in reverse priority for production.** Most agencies build Layer 1 content first because it has the highest volume. The correct order for a new SaaS programme is Layer 3 first, then Layer 2, then Layer 1. This is Pain Point SEO, covered fully in Lesson 2.3.

**Second, each layer requires a different page type and a different brief.** A Layer 1 keyword going into a Layer 3 landing page format produces content that is too product-specific for an awareness-stage reader. A Layer 3 keyword going into a Layer 1 blog post buries a purchase-ready buyer in educational content. The architecture prevents this by assigning layers before any brief is written.

---

**Summary**

- Four layers: problem-aware (Layer 1), category-aware (Layer 2), product-aware (Layer 3), retention (Layer 4).
- Build Layer 3 first. Layer 2 second. Layer 1 third. Layer 4 runs in parallel.
- Each layer requires its own page type and CTA. Mixing them produces content that converts no one well.

## Lesson 2.3 — Layer 1: Problem-Aware Keywords

Layer 1 targets people who have the problem your product solves but do not yet know a software solution exists. They search with problem language, not product language.

**Problem-aware keyword patterns**

| Pattern | Example (for a scheduling SaaS) |
|---|---|
| "How to [do the job manually]" | "how to schedule team shifts in Excel" |
| "Why is [problem happening]" | "why does my team miss schedule updates" |
| "[Problem] without [category]" | "manage remote team schedules without software" |
| "Best way to [accomplish job]" | "best way to coordinate shift changes" |
| "[Problem] + role" | "shift scheduling for small business" |

These keywords have informational intent. The searcher wants to solve a problem, not buy software. The content addresses the problem first and introduces the product as the natural solution.

---

**The JTBD alignment test**

Every Layer 1 keyword passes through the JTBD alignment test from Module 1. The keyword must match at least one job-to-be-done statement in the Product Intelligence Document. If it does not, it is not a Layer 1 keyword for this product. It is a general interest topic with no conversion path.

Grow and Convert documented the conversion consequence of skipping this test. In a test comparing a TOFU guide targeting "what is the net promoter score?" against a BOFU pain point piece on "how to create a survey," the BOFU piece converted dramatically higher than expected. Both seemed like relevant topics for a survey SaaS. The difference was that one matched the buyer's JTBD and the other matched a general interest topic.

---

**Volume expectations and content role**

Layer 1 keywords typically have the highest search volume in a SaaS programme. They are also the lowest-converting. This makes their role in the architecture specific: they build topical authority, bring in top-of-funnel traffic, and internally link to Layer 2 and Layer 3 content.

They do not drive signups on their own. They drive readers toward the pages that do.

Every Layer 1 piece needs at least one internal link to a Layer 2 or Layer 3 page, and a CTA that is appropriate for a first-touch visitor: a free resource, a newsletter, a free tool, or a soft trial offer. Not a demo request.

---

**Common Mistake: Publishing Layer 1 content without a conversion path**

Layer 1 content with no internal links to Layer 2 or Layer 3 and no contextual CTA is dead-end content. It ranks. It drives traffic. It converts nobody. Every Layer 1 article brief must include a mandatory internal link to a Layer 2 or Layer 3 page.

---

**Summary**

- Layer 1 keywords use problem language, not product language. Source them from JTBD statements, support tickets, and G2 reviews.
- Every Layer 1 keyword must pass the JTBD alignment test: it must match a documented job-to-be-done from the Product Intelligence Document.
- Layer 1 content builds authority and drives traffic. It only converts readers if it links to Layer 2 and Layer 3 pages.

## Lesson 2.4 — Layer 2: Category-Aware Keywords

Layer 2 targets people who know software is the solution and are evaluating options. They search with category and comparison language.

**Category-aware keyword patterns**

| Pattern | Example (scheduling SaaS) | Conversion rate |
|---|---|---|
| "Best [category] software" | "best employee scheduling software" | 3-6% |
| "[Category] tools" | "team scheduling tools" | 3-5% |
| "[Competitor] alternatives" | "deputy alternatives" | 6-10% |
| "[Your product] vs [competitor]" | "sling vs homebase" | 8-15% |
| "Best [category] for [use case]" | "best scheduling software for restaurants" | 5-9% |
| "Free [category]" | "free employee scheduling software" | 4-7% |
| "[Category] + business size" | "scheduling software for small business" | 5-8% |

These keywords have commercial investigation intent. The buyer knows they need software. They are comparing options. Your job is to be in the consideration set and to demonstrate why your product fits their specific situation.

---

**The comparison page opportunity**

Six comparison articles, each targeting keywords with fewer than twenty searches per month, combined to produce 149 organic signups, some pages converting at 4.5%. The reported volume for any single "X vs Y" query understates the real opportunity because searchers use multiple phrasings: "X versus Y," "Y versus X," "X or Y," "is X better than Y," "X alternative." Your keyword tool shows each variant separately. Combine them and the actual traffic pool is meaningfully larger.

The conversion premium justifies building comparison and alternative pages even for low-volume keywords. A visitor searching "Salesforce alternatives" is actively evaluating options. The conversion rate on this page should be 8-15%.

---

**The piggyback strategy for small SaaS brands**

Small brands face a specific problem: nobody searches "Your Product vs Competitor" because nobody knows your product yet. The piggyback strategy solves this.

Target comparison keywords between two larger competitors in your category. If your product competes with both OnFleet and Postmates in delivery route optimisation, you can rank for "Postmates vs OnFleet" and capture buyers evaluating both tools who have not yet discovered yours. Your page compares the two honestly and positions your product as the third option worth considering.

This generates qualified traffic from comparison queries where your brand would never otherwise appear.

---

**Common Mistake: Making comparison pages promotional**

A comparison page that is obviously biased toward your product loses the trust of a buyer who is doing independent evaluation. At the evaluation stage, the buyer is sceptical. A genuinely useful comparison that acknowledges competitor strengths converts better than one that lists only competitor weaknesses.

---

**Summary**

- Layer 2 keywords use category and comparison language. Build these before Layer 1 content.
- Comparison and alternative pages convert at 6-15% because they serve buyers in active evaluation mode.
- The piggyback strategy: rank for comparisons between two larger competitors in your space, capturing buyers who have not yet discovered your product.

---

## Lesson 2.5 — Layer 3: Product-Aware Keywords

Layer 3 targets people who are evaluating your specific product. They know what it does. They want to know if it is worth buying.

**Product-aware keyword patterns**

| Pattern | Example | Intent |
|---|---|---|
| "[Product] pricing" | "deputy pricing" | Direct purchase evaluation |
| "[Product] review" | "deputy review" | Validation before purchase |
| "[Product] free trial" | "deputy free trial" | Ready to try |
| "[Product] alternatives" | "deputy alternatives" | Considering switching |
| "[Product] + feature" | "deputy time tracking" | Feature validation |
| "[Product] vs [competitor]" | "deputy vs homebase" | Final comparison |
| "Is [product] worth it" | "is deputy worth it" | Final objection handling |

These keywords have transactional or near-transactional intent. The buyer is one or two steps from a decision.

---

**Build Layer 3 first**

This is the Pain Point SEO principle from Grow and Convert: Pain Point SEO is a strategy describing how to prioritise content ideas around high-intent keywords over high-volume keywords with the goal of driving conversions.

The execution sequence for a new SaaS SEO programme:

1. Build Layer 3 pages first: pricing, trial, demo, feature pages, "vs" and "alternatives" pages.
2. Build Layer 2 content second: category comparison, use-case, and "best [category]" content.
3. Build Layer 1 content third: problem-aware, JTBD-aligned guides and data studies.

This is the opposite of the default agency approach. The reason is compounding. Layer 3 pages convert immediately. The revenue they generate funds the Layer 2 and Layer 1 programmes. A site that starts with Layer 1 content spends six months building traffic with near-zero conversion while the client questions the investment.

---

**Own your branded search before competitors do**

Every Layer 3 keyword is a branded or near-branded query. Your product's pricing, review, and alternatives pages are queries that occur after a buyer has already heard of you. If you do not rank for them, your competitors do.

Companies often ignore high-intent keywords in their space — comparison keywords, jobs-to-be-done keywords, long-tail category variations — because the search volume looks too low, not realising that these keywords convert at much higher rates than high-volume informational terms.

Build your own "[product] pricing," "[product] alternatives," and "[product] vs [competitor]" pages before a competitor or review site owns those queries.

---

**Common Mistake: Treating pricing and trial pages as non-SEO assets**

Most SaaS teams treat the pricing page as a conversion page managed by the product or marketing team, separate from SEO. It is both. The pricing page ranks for "[product] pricing" queries. Its title tag, meta description, and H1 should be optimised. Its content should answer the objections and questions a buyer at this stage has. A pricing page with no SEO consideration loses branded transactional traffic to G2, Capterra, and competitor alternative pages.

---

**Summary**

- Layer 3 keywords are branded and near-branded with transactional intent. Build these first.
- Your own pricing, review, alternatives, and feature pages must rank before competitor review sites and alternatives pages own those queries.
- Pain Point SEO: start at the bottom of the funnel and work upward. The conversion data justifies the sequence.

## Lesson 2.6 — Layer 4: Retention Keywords

Layer 4 targets existing users searching for help, new features, and integrations. It sits outside the acquisition funnel but belongs in the keyword architecture for two reasons.

First, retention content ranks. "How to use [product feature]," "[product] tutorial," and "[product] + [integration]" queries are searched by existing users but also by prospects evaluating whether the product is learnable. A well-structured help centre and knowledge base is an acquisition asset as well as a support asset.

Second, retention keywords signal to Google what the product actually does. A comprehensive knowledge base covering every feature, every integration, and every use case strengthens the site's topical authority on the product category.

**Retention keyword patterns**

| Pattern | Example | Who searches |
|---|---|---|
| "[Product] how to [feature]" | "deputy how to create a schedule" | Existing users + prospects evaluating |
| "[Product] tutorial" | "deputy tutorial" | New users + prospects |
| "[Product] + [integration]" | "deputy quickbooks integration" | Existing users evaluating integrations |
| "[Product] update" | "deputy new features 2025" | Existing users |
| "[Product] troubleshoot" | "deputy schedule not syncing" | Existing users with problems |

Atlassian built the most documented example of retention content crossing into acquisition. Each spoke targets its own keyword cluster while reinforcing the hub's authority on the broader topic. This architecture is how SaaS companies like HubSpot and Ahrefs dominate search results across entire categories. Atlassian's agile resource hub — atlassian.com/agile — ranks for 84,800 keywords and generates 597,000 monthly organic visits, primarily from users searching "how to do agile" content that naturally leads to Jira and Trello.

---

**Common Mistake: Separating the knowledge base from SEO**

Most SaaS companies build their knowledge base on a subdomain (help.product.com) or a third-party tool (Zendesk, Intercom) with no canonical pointing back to the main domain. All the topical authority from help content is lost to the subdomain or the third party. Build the knowledge base on the main domain under /help/ or /docs/ and capture that authority.

---

**Summary**

- Layer 4 keywords target existing users but double as acquisition assets because prospects search them to evaluate product usability.
- Retention content strengthens topical authority on the product category.
- Keep the knowledge base on the main domain. Subdomains and third-party tools lose the authority benefit.

## Lesson 2.7 — Seed Keyword Selection

The seed keyword is the structural decision that determines the shape of the entire topical map. Choose the wrong seed and the topical map produces content for the wrong audience.

**What a seed keyword actually does in SaaS**

The seed keyword is not the keyword you are targeting. It is the input that generates the full keyword universe you research from. A SaaS scheduling tool that enters "scheduling" as its seed gets a keyword universe dominated by general calendar content, date scheduling, and scheduling apps of every kind. A tool that enters "employee scheduling software" gets a universe tightly scoped to its actual buyers.

Three seed keyword types produce three different keyword universes:

| Seed type | Example | Universe scope | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Feature seed | "scheduling" | Too broad — captures unrelated audiences | Diluted topical map |
| Category seed | "employee scheduling software" | Correct scope — captures the buying audience | Works if category is established |
| Problem seed | "shift scheduling for restaurants" | Narrow scope — captures one vertical | Miss other verticals |

For most SaaS companies, the category seed is the right starting point. It matches what buyers search when they know software is the solution. Supplement with problem seeds for each of the top three verticals from the audience profile.

---

**How to identify the right category seed**

1. Pull your best-fit customers from the CRM. Find the segment with the shortest sales cycle and highest retention.
2. Ask five of them: "If you were searching for our product today without knowing our name, what would you type into Google?" Record their exact phrasing.
3. Run each phrase in Ahrefs or SEMrush. The one that generates the most relevant SERP (competitors you recognise, content types that match your product) is your category seed.
4. Do not use your product name as a seed. It only surfaces branded queries.
5. Do not use your company's internal category label. Use what the buyer calls it.

---

**The seed keyword scope test**

Search the candidate seed in Google. Examine the top 10 results. Ask three questions:

1. Are two or more direct competitors ranking? Yes = you are in the right category.
2. Are the results serving the same buyer you are targeting? Yes = correct scope.
3. Does your product belong on this SERP? Yes = correct seed.

Any no means the seed is too broad, too narrow, or misaligned with the actual buyer category.

---

**Common Mistake: Using internal product language as the seed**

A workforce management SaaS calling their seed keyword "workforce optimisation platform" is using language their product team uses, not language their buyers search. The resulting keyword universe produces content nobody outside the company is looking for.

---

**Summary**

- The seed keyword determines the shape of the entire topical map. Choose it based on what buyers type, not what the product team calls the category.
- Category seeds work for established categories. Problem seeds supplement for specific verticals.
- The seed scope test: search the candidate seed and verify competitors are present and the SERP serves your buyer.

## Lesson 2.8 — Long-Tail Strategy for SaaS

Long-tail keywords in SaaS behave differently from long-tail keywords in e-commerce or publishing. Volume is not the primary filter.

**Long-tail SaaS keyword economics**

In B2C content, a long-tail keyword with 500 monthly searches and 3% conversion produces 15 conversions at $15/month = $225/month revenue. The content investment is hard to justify.

In B2B SaaS, a long-tail keyword with 200 monthly searches and 5% conversion produces 10 demo requests. If 20% of demos close at $2,000 ARR, that is $4,000 ARR per month from one article. The economics flip.

The volume-to-value ratio changes entirely when the ticket size changes. Filter long-tail SaaS keywords by intent and job-to-be-done fit, not by volume cutoff.

---

**Five long-tail patterns worth targeting in B2B SaaS**

| Pattern | Example | Why it converts |
|---|---|---|
| "[Category] for [specific industry]" | "scheduling software for healthcare" | Vertical buyers convert faster because they see their context reflected |
| "[Category] for [company size]" | "scheduling software for 50 employees" | Size-specific searches indicate a buyer with a defined procurement context |
| "[Product] + [tech stack]" | "scheduling software that integrates with payroll" | Stack integration is a blocker — solving it signals purchase readiness |
| "[Feature] + [role]" | "shift swap approval for managers" | Role-specific feature searches indicate the practitioner is evaluating daily use |
| "[Category] + [pain outcome]" | "scheduling software to reduce no-shows" | Outcome-specific queries match buyer pain to product value |

---

**How keyword tools undercount long-tail SaaS volume**

Keyword tools report one variant at a time. "Employee scheduling software for restaurants" and "restaurant employee scheduling software" and "best employee scheduling app for restaurants" are reported as separate, low-volume keywords. They are the same searcher, the same intent, and the same buyer. Cluster them before scoring volume.

A keyword cluster of eight variants averaging 150 searches each is a 1,200-search cluster. That changes the prioritisation math significantly.

---

**Common Mistake: Setting a minimum volume floor**

A common agency practice is to filter out keywords under 100 monthly searches. This eliminates the highest-converting BOFU and category-specific clusters in B2B SaaS. Remove the volume floor. Replace it with an intent filter and a conversion value estimate.

---

**Summary**

- Long-tail keyword value in B2B SaaS is determined by ticket size and intent, not by volume.
- Five long-tail patterns drive above-average conversion: vertical-specific, size-specific, stack integration, role-specific feature, and pain-outcome keywords.
- Cluster variant phrasings before scoring volume. Keyword tools undercount long-tail reach by reporting variants separately.

## Lesson 2.9 — Competitor Keyword Mapping

Competitor keywords are the fastest path to Layer 2 conversions. They capture buyers who are already in evaluation mode and have named a competitor as the benchmark.

**Three page types, three keyword structures**

**Type 1: "[Your product] vs [Competitor]"**

This is a purchase-stage keyword. The buyer has narrowed to two options and is making a final comparison. Conversion rates of 10-20% are common for these pages because the intent is the clearest of any keyword type.

Build one page per major competitor. The structure: honest side-by-side comparison, feature table, pricing comparison, who each product is best for, and a clear recommendation. Not a sales page. A genuinely useful comparison that acknowledges competitor strengths where they exist.

URL pattern: /compare/[your-product]-vs-[competitor]/

**Type 2: "Best [Competitor] Alternatives"**

Integration pages and alternative pages rank for high-intent searches from users of those tools. "Best [Competitor] alternatives" captures buyers who are already using the competitor and looking to switch. They are further along the decision journey than a first-time buyer. They know the category. They have pain with the incumbent. They want to leave.

Build one page per competitor you are commonly seen as an alternative to. The structure: brief acknowledgment of what the competitor does well, who it does not suit well, a list of alternatives (include your product and two or three genuine alternatives), and detailed positioning for each.

URL pattern: /alternatives/[competitor]-alternatives/

**Type 3: "Similar tools to [Competitor]"**

Lower intent than Type 1 or 2 but higher volume. Targets buyers in early evaluation who are exploring the category landscape. Useful for category awareness and internal linking to deeper comparison content.

---

**The piggyback competitor strategy at scale**

Circuit is a delivery route optimisation tool. Small brand. No one was searching "Circuit vs OnFleet." But people were searching "Postmates vs OnFleet" — two larger competitors in an adjacent space.

The full approach: identify five to ten competitor-pair comparison searches where both competitors have more brand recognition than your product. Build pages that compare those competitors and position your product as the overlooked alternative worth considering. These pages rank for existing high-volume comparison searches and introduce your product to buyers who would never have found it otherwise.

---

**Competitor keyword research process**

1. List all direct competitors from the positioning work in Module 1.
2. For each competitor, pull their highest-traffic keywords in Ahrefs or SEMrush.
3. Identify which of their keywords are comparison, alternative, or category keywords — not branded navigational.
4. Build the page priority list: highest-volume "vs" and "alternatives" patterns first.
5. Research each competitor's weaknesses from G2, Capterra, and Reddit. These become the honest differentiation points on your comparison pages.

---

**Common Mistake: Publishing biased comparison pages**

A "vs" page that lists only competitor weaknesses signals dishonesty to a buyer doing independent research. The buyer has already read the competitor's marketing. They know the competitor's strengths. An honest page that acknowledges competitor strengths but clearly positions your product for the specific buyer type it suits best converts higher than a page that appears rigged.

---

**Summary**

- Three competitor page types: "vs" pages (highest converting), "alternatives" pages (captures switchers), "similar to" pages (early evaluation stage).
- The piggyback strategy: rank for competitor-pair comparisons to capture buyers who do not yet know your product.
- Honest comparison pages convert better than biased ones. Acknowledge competitor strengths. Position for specific fit.

## Lesson 2.10 — Integration Keyword Opportunities

Integration keywords are one of the highest-value, lowest-competition content categories in SaaS. They capture buyers at the exact moment of a specific technical need.

**Why integration keywords convert**

A buyer searching "[your product] + Salesforce integration" is not browsing. They have a specific blocker. If your product integrates with Salesforce and that integration works well, this buyer is close to signing up. The search query is evidence of purchase intent, not research intent.

Zapier's integration pages target ultra-specific search queries that individually have low search volume but collectively represent massive traffic potential. Zapier built over 50,000 integration pages at the URL pattern /apps/{App1}/integrations/{App2}. By 2024 the site was generating 6 million+ organic visits per month.

Not every SaaS company needs 50,000 pages. But every SaaS company that integrates with five or more tools has an integration keyword opportunity they are almost certainly not capturing.

---

**Integration keyword types and their priority**

| Type | Example | Priority | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct integration | "[product] Salesforce integration" | Highest | Captures stack-specific buyers |
| How-to integration | "how to connect [product] to Slack" | High | Targets users mid-setup |
| Integration comparison | "[product] vs [product] Salesforce integration" | Medium | Captures evaluation-stage buyers |
| Integration workflow | "automate [task] with [product] and [tool]" | Medium | Captures workflow-specific buyers |

---

**The integration page minimum requirements**

A page that just says "Yes, we integrate with Salesforce" does not rank. It does not convert. An integration page that ranks and converts contains:

1. What the integration does in one sentence.
2. The specific workflows it enables (three to five named examples).
3. Step-by-step setup instructions.
4. What data passes between the two systems.
5. Which plan includes the integration.
6. A direct CTA to start the integration or start a trial.

Zapier's integration pages include specific workflow examples, step-by-step setup instructions, popular automation templates, and user testimonials for that specific integration. This depth ensures that even with template-based generation, each page provides genuine value.

---

**Building integration pages at scale**

Integration pages follow a template. Once the template is validated (it ranks, it converts), apply it to every integration in the product. The variable content is the integration name, the workflow examples, and the setup instructions. The page structure stays the same.

This is one of the few legitimate programmatic SEO plays for SaaS. Each integration page serves a genuinely distinct user need with genuinely distinct content. It is not thin content if the workflow examples and setup steps are accurate and specific.

---

**Common Mistake: Building integration pages only for featured partners**

Most SaaS teams build integration pages for their five biggest partners because those are the ones marketing wants to promote. The long-tail integration keywords, "[product] + lesser-known-but-widely-used tool," often have less competition and comparable conversion rates because the buyer with a niche stack has fewer options. Build integration pages for every integration in the product.

---

**Summary**

- Integration keywords signal a specific technical need at or near purchase intent. They convert at rates comparable to comparison keywords.
- Every integration the product supports generates at least one keyword cluster worth targeting.
- Integration pages must include workflow examples, setup instructions, data mapping, and a direct CTA. Thin integration pages do not rank.

## Lesson 2.11 — Navigational Keyword Strategy

Navigational keywords are branded searches by people who already know your product exists. They are among the highest-converting keywords in your entire programme because the searcher has already done some level of research before typing your brand name.

**Navigational keyword types and what happens if you do not own them**

| Keyword | Who searches it | What happens if you do not rank |
|---|---|---|
| "[Product] pricing" | Buyer evaluating cost | G2, Capterra, or a competitor captures them |
| "[Product] review" | Buyer seeking validation | Review sites and competitor alternatives pages capture them |
| "[Product] alternatives" | Buyer considering switching | Competitor alternative pages capture them |
| "[Product] free trial" | Buyer ready to start | They land on your homepage or cannot find the path |
| "[Product] vs [competitor]" | Buyer in final comparison | Competitor's own comparison page ranks above yours |
| "[Product] tutorial" | New user trying to get started | Third-party YouTube or help articles capture them |
| "[Product] login" | Existing user | This is fine, but ensure the page is clean and fast |

If you do not own these pages, your competitors and review sites do. A buyer who types "[product] alternatives" and lands on a competitor's alternative page has just been redirected away from you at the moment of highest intent.

---

**Building the branded SERP**

The goal is to control the top five to eight results for your brand name searches. This requires:

1. A dedicated pricing page with transparent pricing, FAQs, and a strong CTA.
2. A dedicated "[product] alternatives" page on your own site that honestly compares you to alternatives and positions your product for the right buyer.
3. A dedicated "[product] vs [competitor]" page for each major competitor.
4. A dedicated reviews or case studies page that captures "[product] reviews" queries.
5. A structured knowledge base that captures "[product] tutorial" and "[product] how to" searches.

This is not about gaming the SERP. It is about ensuring that the buyer who has already heard of your product and is doing final evaluation research lands on your content, not a competitor's.

---

**Common Mistake: Ignoring navigational keywords in the keyword architecture**

Navigational keywords do not appear in keyword research tools with high difficulty scores because they target your own brand. Teams assume they automatically rank for branded searches. They often do not — review sites, comparison sites, and competitors actively build pages targeting your brand. Audit your branded SERP every quarter.

---

**Summary**

- Navigational keywords convert at the highest rates because the searcher already knows your product. Own them before competitors and review sites do.
- Build dedicated pages for pricing, alternatives, reviews, and "vs" pages under your own domain.
- Audit your branded SERP quarterly. New comparison and alternative pages targeting your brand appear regularly.

## Lesson 2.12 — Keyword Cannibalism in SaaS

Keyword cannibalism in SaaS has a specific pattern that does not appear in standard editorial content. It is not just two pages targeting the same keyword. It is Layer 1 content weakening Layer 3 pages by splitting authority on the same query.

**The TOFU/BOFU cannibalism pattern**

A SaaS company publishes a Layer 1 guide: "How to manage employee schedules." The guide targets "employee scheduling" as its primary keyword. Six months later, the team builds a Layer 3 landing page targeting the same "employee scheduling" keyword for the product-aware buyer.

Now two pages compete for the same primary keyword. The older guide has more links and more traffic history. It outranks the landing page. The result: a Layer 1 educational guide ranks for the primary category keyword instead of the product landing page. Buyers searching the category land on a blog post, not a product page.

The conversion cost is severe. Bottom-of-funnel content converts at 5-7% compared to 1-2% for typical top-of-funnel articles. Every buyer landing on the guide instead of the landing page loses three to six conversion percentage points.

---

**SaaS-specific cannibalism patterns**

| Pattern | What happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Layer 1 guide vs Layer 3 landing page on same category keyword | Guide outranks landing page. Buyers land on educational content. | Redirect guide traffic to landing page or clearly differentiate macro contexts. |
| Two "vs" pages targeting same competitor pair | Both rank weakly. Neither accumulates authority. | Consolidate into one page. 301 redirect the weaker one. |
| Feature page and blog post on same feature | Blog post outranks feature page for feature keywords | Canonicalise blog post to feature page or restructure macro contexts. |
| Multiple "alternatives" pages for same competitor | Same as "vs" cannibalism | Consolidate and redirect. |
| Pricing page and "how much does X cost" blog post | Blog post captures pricing queries instead of pricing page | Clarify macro contexts. Blog post should answer context, link to pricing page. Pricing page should own the direct query. |

---

**Detection process**

1. Pull all URLs from the site.
2. For each Core Layer 2 and Layer 3 keyword cluster, run: site:yourdomain.com [keyword] in Google.
3. If more than one URL appears, check both pages' rankings in Search Console for the shared keyword.
4. Flag any case where a Layer 1 page outranks a Layer 2 or Layer 3 page for the same core keyword.

---

**Resolution**

- If the Layer 1 page is generating significant traffic but the wrong conversions: add a clear CTA linking to the Layer 3 page. Differentiate the macro contexts so each page owns a distinct SERP.
- If the Layer 1 page is thin and not converting: consolidate into the Layer 3 page, add educational depth to the Layer 3 page, 301 redirect the Layer 1 URL.
- If two same-layer pages compete: identify which has more authority, consolidate content into the stronger page, 301 redirect the weaker.

---

**Common Mistake: Creating "hub" pages that dilute Layer 3 landing pages**

Some teams create a pillar page that covers the entire product category broadly. It targets every keyword variation in the category simultaneously. The pillar page outranks specific feature and landing pages for the keywords those pages were built to own. The result is a hub page that ranks for everything and converts nothing because its macro context is too broad for any specific buying stage.

Give every page one macro context. Layer 1 pages own problem-aware queries. Layer 3 pages own product-aware queries. They must not compete.

---

**Summary**

- SaaS cannibalism most often occurs when Layer 1 guides and Layer 3 landing pages target the same primary category keyword.
- Detection: search site:domain.com [keyword] and check Search Console for shared queries across multiple pages.
- Resolution: consolidate weaker pages, 301 redirect, or clearly differentiate macro contexts so each page serves one intent without competing.

## Module 2 Summary

Three things govern every keyword decision in a SaaS programme:

- **Funnel layer before volume.** Assign every keyword a layer (1-4) before scoring it for volume. Layer 3 keywords with 200 monthly searches are worth more than Layer 1 keywords with 20,000 monthly searches for most B2B SaaS companies.
- **Build bottom-up.** Layer 3 first, Layer 2 second, Layer 1 third. The conversion data justifies this sequence. Bottom-of-funnel content converts at 5-7%. Top-of-funnel converts at under 2%.
- **Prevent cannibalism before it starts.** Assign one macro context per page and one funnel layer per keyword cluster before any brief is written. Cannibalism is a planning failure, not a content failure.
