# SaaS SEO Course
## Module 6 — BOFU Content: Capturing Signup-Ready Buyers

**Prerequisite:** Modules 1-5 complete.
**Module outcome:** A BOFU Landing Page Brief set — pricing page, free trial page, demo page, and top integration landing pages optimised for both ranking and conversion.

---

## Why BOFU Comes First in the Production Sequence

Bottom-of-funnel keywords drive 40-60% of organic SaaS conversions. A buyer searching "[product] pricing" or "free trial [product]" is days away from a decision. They already know the product. They want the final piece of information before committing.

Yet most SaaS teams build these pages last, after the blog content programme is running. The result: the site has 50 educational articles and a pricing page that was designed by the product team three years ago, has no title tag optimisation, and loses branded search traffic to G2 and Capterra every day.

The correct sequence from Module 2 is: Layer 3 first, Layer 2 second, Layer 1 third. This module builds the Layer 3 pages — the pages that close buyers who are already interested — before anything else.

---

## Lesson 6.1 — The BOFU Keyword Set

BOFU keywords are low-volume, high-intent, and predominantly branded or near-branded. They are the queries a buyer types when they are one or two steps from a signup decision.

**The BOFU keyword types**

| Type | Pattern | Example | Who searches |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pricing intent | "[product] pricing" | "deputy pricing" | Buyer evaluating cost before committing |
| Trial intent | "[product] free trial" | "deputy free trial" | Buyer ready to test |
| Demo intent | "[product] demo" | "deputy demo" | Enterprise buyer seeking a guided evaluation |
| Feature validation | "[product] [feature]" | "deputy time tracking" | Buyer confirming a required capability |
| Review intent | "[product] review" | "deputy review" | Buyer seeking third-party validation |
| Integration intent | "[product] [tool] integration" | "deputy quickbooks" | Buyer confirming technical fit |
| Signup intent | "[product] sign up" | "deputy sign up" | Buyer at the door |

**Why Google's data underreports BOFU volume**

Keyword tools report "[product] pricing" as 400 monthly searches. The actual qualified audience is larger because:
- Buyers search the same intent with multiple phrasings: "how much does deputy cost," "deputy price per user," "deputy plans and pricing"
- Many branded BOFU searches happen in browsers already navigating to the site — they never enter Google
- Referral traffic from review sites lands on the same page the pricing query targets

The conversion value per visitor is the correct filter, not raw volume. A 400-search/month pricing page keyword with a 15% conversion rate to trial, and a 20% trial-to-paid conversion, produces 12 new customers per month from organic search alone.

**Build BOFU pages before competitors and review sites own the queries**

Every BOFU keyword is also searchable by buyers who found the product through any other channel — paid, referral, word-of-mouth. If your pricing page ranks first for "[product] pricing," every buyer regardless of source arrives at your page. If you do not rank for it, G2, Capterra, or a competitor's alternatives page captures them at the moment of highest intent.

---

**Common Mistake: Treating BOFU pages as non-SEO assets**

Pricing, trial, and demo pages are conversion pages managed by the product or marketing team. They often have no title tag, no meta description, and no consideration for the "[product] pricing" query that a buyer types immediately after deciding to evaluate the product. These pages must be optimised for search simultaneously with conversion.

---

**Summary**

- BOFU keywords are low-volume and high-intent. Volume is the wrong filter. Conversion value per visitor is the correct one.
- Build BOFU pages first. They capture buyers who arrive from any channel — paid, referral, or organic.
- Every BOFU page needs title tag, meta description, and on-page SEO alongside the conversion architecture.

*Next: The pricing page is the highest-converting page on most SaaS sites. It requires both SEO and content optimisation.*

## Lesson 6.2 — Pricing Page SEO

The pricing page has two jobs. It must rank for "[product] pricing" queries. It must convert the buyer who lands on it.

Most pricing pages fail at the first job because nobody applied SEO. They fail at the second job because they lead with price instead of value.

**Pricing page SEO requirements**

Title tag: "[Product Name] Pricing — Plans from $[lowest price]/month"
- Include the product name, the word "pricing," and the lowest entry price if publicly available
- Under 60 characters

Meta description: "Compare [Product Name] plans for [audience type]. [Key feature differentiator]. Start free with no credit card required."
- Include "pricing," the buyer context, one key differentiator, and the no-risk CTA
- Under 160 characters

H1: Match the buyer's actual question — not "Our Pricing" but "[Product Name] Pricing: Find the Right Plan for Your Team"

URL: /pricing/ — always at root level, one click from homepage

Schema: Use Product or Offer schema for each pricing tier where applicable. This enables price display in rich results for some queries.

**Pricing page content requirements for ranking**

Google ranks pricing pages based on content quality, not just brand authority. A pricing page that contains only a price table ranks below one that answers the questions buyers have when evaluating the price.

The content sections a ranking pricing page needs:

1. **Value statement above the table.** One sentence: what the buyer gets for the price. Not "Simple, transparent pricing." "Schedule, manage, and pay your entire hourly workforce from one platform, starting at $3.50 per user per month."

2. **Plan comparison table.** Transparent, specific feature breakdown per tier. No "contact sales" in place of prices for SMB tiers — buyers abandon pages that hide SMB pricing.

3. **FAQ section.** Five to eight questions sourced from sales call recordings. "What happens at the end of the free trial?" "Is there a per-user fee?" "Do you offer annual discounts?" "What's included in the free plan?" These are the questions buyers type into Google after seeing the price. Answering them on the page captures those queries and reduces the friction that leads to abandonment.

4. **Social proof.** One to two customer quotes specific to pricing value — "We expected enterprise software to be out of reach but [product] costs less than our previous Excel workaround when you count staff time."

5. **ROI statement or calculator.** For B2B SaaS with cycle lengths over 30 days: one paragraph or an interactive calculator that frames the price as a return on investment. HubSpot's pricing pages include "Teams using HubSpot Marketing Hub see an average 129% increase in leads generated." This is not a promise — it is a framing device that changes how the buyer evaluates the cost.

**Pricing page psychology: lead with outcomes, not costs**

HubSpot's 2024 pricing page analysis found that pages that lead with outcomes convert 34% better than pages that lead with features. A pricing page that opens with "Here are our three plans: Starter, Professional, Enterprise" is feature-led. A pricing page that opens with "Here is what [product] produces for teams like yours, at three investment levels" is outcome-led.

The buyer is not evaluating a cost. They are evaluating a return. Frame accordingly.

---

**Common Mistake: Hiding pricing behind "contact sales" for mid-market tiers**

67% of pricing page abandonment happens when buyers suspect hidden costs. A buyer at the SMB or early mid-market stage who sees "Contact Sales" instead of a price does not contact sales. They go to a review site to find out what the product costs. If the pricing is competitive, hiding it loses deals. Publish pricing for every tier where it is not genuinely custom.

---

**Summary**

- Title tag includes product name, "pricing," and lowest public price. Meta description includes buyer context and no-risk CTA.
- Content sections that enable ranking: value statement, plan comparison table, FAQ, social proof, ROI statement.
- Lead with outcomes. Pages that lead with value convert 34% better than pages that lead with features or cost.

## Lesson 6.3 — Free Trial and Demo Pages

The free trial page and the demo page serve different buyer types. The brief for each requires different architecture.

**Free trial page: PLG and B2C SaaS**

The free trial page targets buyers who are ready to test the product independently. It serves PLG and B2C SaaS where the buyer makes the decision without a sales conversation.

SEO target keywords: "[product] free trial," "[product] try free," "sign up [product]"

**Free trial page requirements:**

Title tag: "[Product Name] Free Trial — Start in 2 Minutes, No Credit Card"
H1: "Try [Product Name] Free for [X] Days"
Body content: Minimum 200 words. Not a wall of text — five to seven bullet points answering: what is included in the trial, how long it lasts, whether a card is required, what happens when it ends, and what the buyer gets in the first week.

The body content exists for two reasons: it answers the questions buyers search for before clicking "start free trial," and it provides enough text for Google to understand the page's intent.

CTA: "Start free trial — no credit card required." The no-credit-card statement is not optional. A 2024 SaaS benchmark study found that removing credit card requirements from trial pages increases sign-up rates by 17-25%. State it explicitly.

**Demo page: B2B and enterprise SaaS**

The demo page targets enterprise and mid-market buyers who want a guided evaluation before committing. It serves buyers in 30-day-plus sales cycles who will not make a decision without speaking to someone.

SEO target keywords: "[product] demo," "schedule [product] demo," "[product] demo request"

**Demo page requirements:**

Title tag: "[Product Name] Demo — See It in Action for Your Team"
H1: "Book a [Product Name] Demo: 30-Minute Personalised Walkthrough"

Body content — the three sections that increase demo page conversion:

1. **What happens in the demo.** Exact agenda. "In your 30-minute demo, you will see: how to build your first schedule in under 5 minutes, how compliance rules apply automatically, and how the manager app works on mobile." Specificity reduces friction. Buyers cancel demo requests when they do not know what they are signing up for.

2. **Who it is for.** Explicitly state the buyer type. "This demo is designed for operations managers, HR leads, and business owners managing hourly workforces of 20+ people." Buyers who are not the right fit self-select out. Buyers who match the description convert.

3. **Social proof from the demo experience.** "9 out of 10 teams who completed a demo implemented [product] within 2 weeks." Or a customer quote about the demo experience specifically.

Form: First name, last name, work email, company size, and one qualifying question relevant to the product ("How many employees do you schedule?"). No more than five fields. Every additional field reduces form completion by approximately 10%.

---

**Common Mistake: Demo pages that open with a form and nothing else**

A demo request form with no surrounding context converts poorly because the buyer has not yet been given a reason to fill it in. The buyer landing on the demo page from an organic search has found a form with no explanation of what happens next, who gets in touch, or what the demo covers. Build the three content sections before the form. The form at the bottom of an informed, relevant page converts higher than the same form with no context.

---

**Summary**

- Free trial pages target PLG/B2C buyers. Include what is in the trial, the duration, and the no-credit-card statement explicitly.
- Demo pages target B2B/enterprise buyers. Three sections before the form: demo agenda, who it is for, social proof from the demo itself.
- No-credit-card statements increase trial sign-up rates by 17-25%. State it explicitly on every trial page.

## Lesson 6.4 — The Zapier Model: BOFU Landing Pages at Scale

The pricing, trial, and demo pages are fixed assets. They exist once each. The BOFU opportunity extends further through use-case-specific and integration-specific landing pages that each target a distinct Layer 3 keyword cluster.

Zapier's integration pages are the reference model for Layer 3 at scale. Zapier built 50,000+ integration pages at /apps/{App1}/integrations/{App2}/. These are not TOFU educational content. They are Layer 3 landing pages targeting buyers who are searching "[tool] integration" — a query that appears at the point of technical evaluation, one step before signup.

**Use-case specific BOFU pages**

Beyond the integration architecture, use-case specific BOFU pages target buyers who have defined their exact context and are searching for a product that fits it.

Query pattern: "[category] for [specific use case]" — "scheduling software for 100+ employees," "employee scheduling with payroll integration," "scheduling software with open shift feature"

These pages are Layer 3 because the specificity signals a buyer who has evaluated multiple options and is now confirming fit for their exact situation.

The difference from Layer 2 use-case pages: Layer 2 use-case pages address a broad vertical or industry. Layer 3 use-case pages address a specific configuration, feature requirement, or integration need that a buyer has after they have narrowed their evaluation.

| Layer 2 use-case page | Layer 3 use-case page |
|---|---|
| "Scheduling software for restaurants" | "Scheduling software for restaurant groups with 20+ locations" |
| "HR software for small business" | "HR software with built-in payroll for under-50-person companies" |
| "CRM for marketing agencies" | "CRM for marketing agencies with client portal and project tracking" |

The Layer 3 version targets a buyer who knows exactly what they need. The conversion rate is higher because the match is more precise.

**Building the BOFU landing page programme**

1. Pull search query data from Google Search Console for all queries that land on the pricing or trial page.
2. Identify Layer 3 queries with 50+ monthly impressions that the pricing or trial page ranks for but not in the top five.
3. Build dedicated Layer 3 landing pages for each of these queries.
4. Each page targets one specific query, one specific buyer context, and one CTA.

Example: The pricing page receives impressions for "scheduling software with payroll integration under $10 per user." It ranks at position 18. A dedicated page at /for/scheduling-with-payroll-integration/ targeting this query converts better than the generic pricing page because it addresses the specific buyer concern.

---

**Common Mistake: Sending all BOFU queries to the pricing page**

The pricing page has one macro context: price evaluation. A buyer searching "scheduling software with payroll integration" wants to know if the product integrates with their payroll system, what that integration covers, and what it costs. The pricing page answers the last question only. A dedicated Layer 3 page answers all three and then links to the pricing page for the final cost evaluation. Dedicated pages for specific BOFU queries convert better than the pricing page for any query that is about more than price.

---

**Summary**

- Use-case specific BOFU pages target buyers who have narrowed to a specific configuration need. They convert above the generic pricing page.
- Pull Search Console data from the pricing and trial pages to identify Layer 3 queries that need dedicated pages.
- Zapier's integration architecture is the scale model: each integration page is a Layer 3 asset targeting a specific buyer at evaluation stage.

## Lesson 6.5 — Objection Handling Through Content

In B2B SaaS with cycles longer than 30 days, buyers encounter blockers that stop deals from closing. They are not product objections. They are procurement objections: security concerns, compliance requirements, implementation complexity, total cost of ownership, and integration fit.

Content that pre-answers these objections removes them before they reach the sales team. It also ranks for the queries buyers use when researching these concerns.

**The five B2B procurement objection categories**

| Objection | Buyer query | Content type |
|---|---|---|
| Security and compliance | "[product] SOC 2," "[product] GDPR," "[product] data security" | Dedicated security page |
| Implementation complexity | "how long does [product] implementation take," "[product] onboarding" | Implementation guide page |
| Total cost of ownership | "[product] ROI," "cost of [product] vs current process" | ROI calculator or TCO page |
| Integration fit | "[product] [system] integration" | Integration page (Module 3 Lesson 3.5) |
| Support and reliability | "[product] uptime," "[product] support," "[product] SLA" | Trust/support page |

**The security page: mandatory for mid-market and enterprise**

Any SaaS product selling to companies with 50+ employees will encounter security reviews. A procurement team will search "[product] SOC 2," "[product] security," and "[product] data processing agreement" before approving a purchase.

A dedicated security page at /security/ covering:
- Compliance certifications (SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA, ISO 27001 — list only what is actually certified)
- Data storage and processing (geography, encryption standards, access controls)
- Penetration testing cadence
- Business continuity and uptime commitments
- DPA availability and download link

This page ranks for all security-related branded queries and removes a procurement blocker that adds two to four weeks to enterprise sales cycles. It also signals trust to mid-market buyers who evaluate security before purchasing.

**The ROI page: mandatory for any B2B SaaS with mid-market or enterprise customers**

An ROI page or calculator addresses the "is this worth the cost" objection that every CFO or budget approver encounters. It does not need to be a complex interactive tool. A structured page with:

1. The typical cost of the problem the product solves (in hours, errors, or direct costs)
2. The typical reduction in that cost after implementation (with specific customer data)
3. A simple calculation framework the buyer can apply to their own numbers
4. Three to five customer quotes with specific metrics

This page ranks for "[product] ROI," "ROI of [category]," and "cost of [manual process] vs [category]" queries. It also functions as a champion enablement asset — buyers use it to justify the purchase internally to their CFO.

---

**Common Mistake: Building objection-handling content after the sales team reports losing deals**

By the time the sales team reports losing deals due to security concerns or ROI uncertainty, the product has already lost prospects who never reached the sales conversation. Most enterprise buyers complete 60-70% of their evaluation before contacting sales. Objection-handling content works on the buyers who never become leads. Build it before the sales team asks for it.

---

**Summary**

- Five B2B procurement objection categories each require dedicated content: security, implementation, ROI, integration, and support/reliability.
- The security page removes a blocker that adds two to four weeks to enterprise sales cycles. Build it before the first enterprise deal.
- ROI pages function as both organic search assets and internal sales tools. Buyers use them to justify purchases to CFOs.

## Lesson 6.6 — Measuring BOFU Performance

BOFU pages are the simplest layer to measure. The conversion event is specific: a trial signup, a demo request, or a contact form submission. The source is identifiable: the page URL in Google Analytics.

But most agencies measure BOFU incorrectly because they attribute conversion to the last page visited, not to the content that drove the decision.

**The correct BOFU measurement model**

Three metrics per BOFU page:

1. **Organic sessions.** From Google Analytics: users arriving from organic search specifically. Not all sessions.

2. **Conversion rate.** Signups or demo requests divided by organic sessions. The benchmark: pricing pages convert at 10-20% for warm branded traffic, 3-8% for cold category traffic. Trial pages convert at 15-30% for branded traffic.

3. **Assisted conversion attribution.** The BOFU page may not be the last page before signup. A buyer may read a comparison article (MOFU), land on the pricing page, leave, and return directly later to sign up. Google Analytics 4's multi-touch attribution shows the BOFU page's role in the conversion path even when it is not the last touchpoint.

**The reporting structure for SaaS clients**

Clients do not want to hear about rankings. They want to hear about signups. The monthly SaaS SEO report contains:

| Metric | Source | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Organic signups this month | GA4 organic conversion events | The primary KPI |
| BOFU page organic sessions | GA4 organic traffic by page | Leading indicator for signups |
| BOFU page conversion rates | GA4 conversion rate by page | Quality indicator — dropping rate signals a page problem |
| MOFU page organic sessions | GA4 organic traffic by page | Pipeline builder |
| TOFU page organic sessions | GA4 organic traffic by page | Awareness indicator |
| Top 5 ranking improvements | Search Console | Explanation for traffic changes |
| Pages entering top 10 this month | Search Console | Forward-looking signal |

The report leads with organic signups. Rankings are supporting context, not the primary metric.

**Attribution for long B2B sales cycles**

A B2B SaaS buyer with a 90-day cycle may interact with five to eight content pieces before requesting a demo. Last-click attribution credits the demo page. First-click attribution credits whatever they read first. Neither is accurate.

The correct model for reporting to B2B SaaS clients: use GA4's path exploration to identify the most common content journeys before a demo request. Report the pattern: "Buyers who request a demo most commonly read our [comparison page] and [wattage guide equivalent] before booking. Organic conversions from these two pages accounted for 47% of demo requests this month."

This gives clients the content attribution story they can use internally to justify the SEO investment, without requiring a complex multi-touch attribution infrastructure.

---

**Common Mistake: Reporting organic traffic as the primary KPI**

Traffic growth looks good in a slide deck. It produces no revenue on its own. A SaaS SEO programme that grows traffic by 40% while signups stay flat has optimised for the wrong metric. Report signups first. Report traffic as the explanation for signup growth or as the leading indicator for future signup growth. Never the primary KPI.

---

**Summary**

- Three metrics per BOFU page: organic sessions, conversion rate, and assisted conversion attribution.
- Report organic signups first. Rankings and traffic are supporting context.
- Use GA4 path exploration to identify common content journeys before conversion events. This is the attribution story clients use internally.

## Module 6 Summary

Three rules govern every BOFU decision:

- **Build BOFU before TOFU.** Two BOFU articles per week will drive more trials in months 1-6 than 10 TOFU articles. The conversion rate gap (BOFU at 10-20%, TOFU at under 2%) makes this non-negotiable for early-stage SaaS programmes.
- **Every BOFU page needs SEO.** Pricing, trial, and demo pages managed by product teams without SEO consideration lose branded search traffic to G2, Capterra, and competitor alternative pages every day. Apply the same title tag, meta description, and content standards to BOFU pages as to any other page on the site.
- **Measure signups, not traffic.** The BOFU content programme is justified by organic signup attribution, not by organic traffic growth. Build the measurement structure before the content programme begins.
