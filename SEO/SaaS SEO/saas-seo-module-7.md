## Module 7 — Competitor and Alternative Page Strategy

**Prerequisite:** Modules 1-6 complete.
**Module outcome:** A competitor page template set and a competitor keyword gap matrix — production-ready for the highest-converting content category in SaaS SEO.

## Why This Module Exists as a Standalone

Competitor and alternative pages were introduced in Module 2 (Lesson 2.9) and Module 5 (Lessons 5.2 and 5.3). This module goes deeper because the stakes are higher than the earlier lessons could cover.

Comparison and alternatives keywords convert at over 7.5%. Mentimeter's single "Kahoot Alternatives" page produced 1,700 sign-ups. The Circuit/piggyback model generates qualified pipeline from competitor-pair queries where the product was never part of the original search.

These pages also decay the fastest, require the most careful legal and ethical handling, and carry the highest risk of trust damage if executed poorly. A module that treats them as one lesson among many does not do justice to their complexity or their value.

This module covers what the earlier lessons did not: the legal boundaries, the at-scale maintenance protocol, the piggyback strategy in operational detail, and the graph method for finding competitor keyword gaps before competitors do.

## Lesson 7.1 — The Three Page Types and Their Buyer Positions

Three distinct page types serve competitor keyword intent. Each targets a buyer at a different position in the decision process. They are not interchangeable.

**Type 1: "[Your product] vs [Competitor]"**

The buyer has narrowed to two options. This is the final comparison stage. They know both products. They want the tiebreaker.

Buyer position: days to one or two weeks from a decision.
Conversion rate: 8–15%.
Priority: Build for every competitor you commonly encounter in sales.
URL pattern: /compare/[your-product]-vs-[competitor]/

**Type 2: "Best [Competitor] Alternatives"**

The buyer has evaluated or used the competitor and is looking to switch. They have a specific pain point with the incumbent.

Buyer position: days to two weeks from a decision.
Conversion rate: 6–10%.
Priority: Build for every competitor whose dissatisfied users are your best prospects.
URL pattern: /alternatives/[competitor]-alternatives/

**Type 3: "Similar tools to [Competitor]" or "Tools like [Competitor]"**

The buyer is in early category exploration. They know one tool and are discovering what else exists. This is a higher-volume, lower-intent query than Types 1 and 2.

Buyer position: one to four weeks from a decision.
Conversion rate: 3–6%.
Priority: Build once the Type 1 and Type 2 pages are live and ranking.
URL pattern: /alternatives/tools-like-[competitor]/

**Building priority order**

1. Type 1 pages for competitors you most commonly lose deals to.
2. Type 2 pages for competitors whose user pain points most closely match your product's strengths.
3. Type 3 pages for high-volume competitors in your category after Types 1 and 2 are established.

Source the priority order from the sales team's "why we lost" notes and the CRM's competitor-attributed loss reasons. The competitors causing the most deal losses are the ones worth addressing first.

---

**Common Mistake: Building all three types simultaneously before any are ranking**

Teams that launch 15 competitor pages in one sprint dilute production quality across all of them. Build the top three Type 1 pages first. Get them ranking. Validate the template converts. Then expand.

---

**Summary**

- Three page types, three distinct buyer positions, three different conversion rate ranges.
- Build in priority order: Type 1 first (highest conversion, most strategic), Type 2 second, Type 3 third.
- Source competitor priority from sales loss data, not from search volume alone.

## Lesson 7.2 — Competitor Keyword Research and the Volume Stacking Method

Keyword tools report competitor-related keywords as individual, often low-volume terms. "Competitor vs product" shows 200 searches. "Product vs competitor" shows 150. "Is competitor better than product" shows 80. Teams see these numbers and deprioritise the pages.

The correct analysis stacks all variants of the same intent into one cluster before scoring.

**The volume stacking process**

1. Enter the competitor name into Ahrefs or SEMrush.
2. Filter for keywords containing your product name or category alongside the competitor name.
3. Add all intent variations:
   - "[competitor] vs [product]"
   - "[product] vs [competitor]"
   - "[competitor] or [product]"
   - "[competitor] alternative to [product]"
   - "is [competitor] better than [product]"
   - "[competitor] compared to [product]"
   - "[competitor] [product] comparison"
4. Sum the volume across all variants.
5. Apply a 1.5–2x multiplier for variants the tool does not detect (browser navigations, zero-volume tracked variants).

A cluster that looks like 200 searches per month from one query is often 800–1,200 when all variants are stacked. That changes the investment decision.

**The "alternatives" cluster stacking**

For Type 2 pages, add:
- "best [competitor] alternatives"
- "[competitor] alternatives free"
- "[competitor] alternatives [year]"
- "[competitor] alternative for [use case]"
- "switch from [competitor]"
- "[competitor] replacement"
- "tools like [competitor]"
- "similar to [competitor]"

A competitor with 300 searches for "best [competitor] alternatives" often has 2,000–4,000 total searches when all switching-intent variants are clustered. One page captures all of them.

**The graph method: plotting competitor opportunity**

Map competitors on a two-axis matrix before deciding which to prioritise:

X-axis: Estimated total stacked search volume for all competitor-related keywords.
Y-axis: Estimated buying intent score (based on conversion rate benchmarks for query type).

Plot every competitor in the category. The competitors in the top-right quadrant (high volume, high intent) are the highest-priority pages.

| Competitor | Stacked volume | Intent score | Quadrant | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Competitor A | 4,200/month | High (vs + alternatives mix) | Top right | 1 |
| Competitor B | 1,800/month | High (mostly "vs" queries) | Top right | 2 |
| Competitor C | 6,000/month | Medium (mostly "tools like") | Top left | 3 |
| Competitor D | 800/month | High (mostly "alternatives") | Bottom right | 4 |

Competitor C has the most volume but lower intent queries. Competitor A has high volume and high intent — build that page first.

---

**Common Mistake: Using single-keyword volume to prioritise competitor pages**

A competitor with a single "vs" query at 200 searches and eight "alternatives" variants collectively at 2,400 searches looks low-priority from the single-keyword view and high-priority from the stacked view. Always stack before scoring. Single-keyword volume underestimates competitor page opportunity by a factor of three to five in most SaaS categories.

---

**Summary**

- Stack all intent variants before scoring competitor keyword volume. Single-query volume underestimates opportunity by 3–5x.
- The graph method: plot competitors by stacked volume (X-axis) and intent score (Y-axis). Build top-right quadrant first.
- Competitor priority = high stacked volume + high intent score. Not single-keyword volume alone.

## Lesson 7.3 — Legal and Ethical Boundaries

Competitor pages are the highest-risk content type for legal exposure. Trademark claims, defamation risks, and false advertising regulations all apply. Most SaaS teams are unaware of the specific lines until they cross one.

**The trademark use rule**

Using a competitor's name in content is generally lawful under "nominative use" doctrine: you can name a competitor to identify which product you are comparing, not to imply endorsement, affiliation, or confusion with the competitor's brand.

What is allowed: "This page compares [Your Product] with [Competitor] to help buyers evaluate both options."

What is not allowed: Using the competitor's logo, using branded colour schemes or visual elements that could imply affiliation, using the competitor's name in your domain, or implying the competitor endorses your product.

**The false claim rule**

Every claim about a competitor on a comparison or alternatives page must be verifiable from public sources. Pricing claims from the competitor's own pricing page. Feature claims from the competitor's own feature documentation, G2 reviews, or Capterra listings.

What is allowed: "As of [date], [Competitor]'s Starter plan is priced at $[X]/user/month based on their published pricing page."

What is not allowed: "Competitor's software crashes frequently" unless this is documented from third-party verified sources. Unverifiable negative claims about reliability, security, or quality are defamation risk.

**The date-stamp rule**

Competitor pages must carry a "last updated" date and must be reviewed quarterly. A comparison page with stale pricing or missing new features is not just a trust problem — it is a false advertising problem if a buyer relies on it to make a decision and the information is wrong.

**The attribution rule**

Every competitor data point on the page has a source. Pricing from the competitor's pricing page (URL + date). Feature data from G2 (link to the listing). Review quotes from Capterra (link to the specific review). This protects against defamation claims and demonstrates good faith if a competitor ever challenges the content.

Document sources in the brief. Maintain them in a competitor data spreadsheet. Update them at every quarterly review.

---

**What to do when a competitor sends a cease and desist**

It happens. Prepare the response before it arrives.

1. Review the specific claim. Is the claimed inaccuracy actually inaccurate? If yes, correct it and respond acknowledging the correction.
2. If the claim is accurate and legally defensible, obtain legal review before removing content.
3. Do not remove accurate, well-sourced content under competitive pressure without legal review. Competitors sometimes send C&D letters as a tactic, not because the content is actually unlawful.
4. Add a disclosure to every competitor page: "This comparison reflects information available as of [date]. We update this page quarterly. If you believe any information is inaccurate, contact us at [email]."

---

**Common Mistake: Publishing competitor pricing without a date stamp and source attribution**

A competitor changes their pricing. Your page still shows the old pricing with no date. A buyer makes a decision based on your page and finds the pricing is different. The competitor has a legitimate complaint. Every pricing data point on every competitor page needs a date stamp and a source URL.

---

**Summary**

- Nominative use: naming a competitor to identify them is lawful. Implying affiliation, using their logo, or making unverifiable negative claims is not.
- Every competitor claim is attributed to a verifiable public source with a date.
- Quarterly review is not optional — stale information creates both trust and legal risk.

## Lesson 7.4 — The Piggyback Strategy

The piggyback strategy targets comparison queries between two larger competitors in your category. Your product is not in the query. Both competitors have more brand recognition. You build the page anyway and introduce your product as the overlooked third option.

**Why it works**

A buyer searching "Postmates vs OnFleet" is evaluating delivery route optimisation software. They are in the evaluation stage. They are going to buy something. They are looking for the difference between two options they have already shortlisted. Your page that honestly compares those two and introduces Circuit as a third option is genuinely useful to this buyer.

The buyer did not know your product existed. They find it at the point of highest evaluation intent. Conversion rates from piggyback pages can match Type 1 "vs" pages because the buyer is in the same evaluation mindset.

**How to identify piggyback opportunities**

1. List every direct competitor in your category (from the competitive alternatives section of the Product Intelligence Document).
2. For every pair of competitors, check: do they have a common "vs" query with 200+ monthly searches?
3. Build a 3×3 matrix of competitor-pair comparison volume.
4. Identify the pairs with the highest combined stacked volume.
5. Build piggyback pages for the top three competitor pairs with genuine product fit.

**Competitor pair priority matrix for a scheduling SaaS:**

| Competitor pair | Stacked volume | Your product fit | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deputy vs Homebase | 2,800/month | High — directly comparable | 1 |
| Sling vs When I Work | 1,600/month | High — same buyer type | 2 |
| Connecteam vs Homebase | 900/month | Medium — overlapping features | 3 |
| Deputy vs 7shifts | 700/month | Medium — overlapping features | 4 |

**The piggyback page structure**

```
H1: [Competitor A] vs [Competitor B]: Which Is Better for [Buyer Type]?

Opening (2 paragraphs):
  - What each product does (neutral)
  - Why buyers compare these two specifically

Side-by-side comparison table (Competitor A vs Competitor B)
  - 8–10 features most relevant to this buyer
  - Honest ratings for both

Where [Competitor A] wins:
  - 2–3 genuine advantages with specifics

Where [Competitor B] wins:
  - 2–3 genuine advantages with specifics

Who should choose [Competitor A]:
  - Specific buyer type

Who should choose [Competitor B]:
  - Specific buyer type

[Your Product] as a third option:
  - Brief introduction: what makes it different from both
  - Which buyer type it suits better than either competitor
  - Key differentiators not present in either competitor
  - CTA: "See how [Your Product] compares" (links to your /compare/ page
         or the pricing page)
  - One customer quote

Conclusion:
  - Summary recommendation matrix (one line per product per buyer type)
```

**The honest positioning requirement on piggyback pages**

The piggyback page earns its ranking and conversion by being genuinely useful. Buyers who searched for Competitor A vs Competitor B are not looking for your product's sales pitch. They are looking for an honest comparison of the two products they were already evaluating.

The comparison between Competitor A and Competitor B must be genuinely honest — not slanted to make both look bad and your product look good by contrast. Buyers read this pattern immediately. The introduction of your product must be as a genuinely relevant third option, not as the predetermined winner.

---

**Common Mistake: Building piggyback pages for competitor pairs with no product fit**

A workflow automation SaaS building a piggyback page for "Excel vs Google Sheets" targets a buyer who is evaluating spreadsheet software, not automation software. The product has no natural fit at the point where the buyer is evaluating. The third-option introduction fails because there is no genuine need bridge. Piggyback pages only work when your product is a genuine alternative for the buyer making the comparison — not just an adjacent tool you want to introduce.

---

**Summary**

- Piggyback pages target competitor-pair comparison queries. Your product is introduced as the third option.
- Competitor pair priority: high stacked volume + genuine product fit for the buyer making that specific comparison.
- Honest comparison between the two competitors is required. Slanting the comparison to make both look bad destroys the trust that makes the third-option introduction work.

## Lesson 7.5 — The Competitor Page Maintenance Protocol

Competitor pages are the most maintenance-intensive content type in SaaS SEO. Competitors change pricing, launch features, get acquired, or shut down. A page that was accurate at launch becomes inaccurate within weeks without a maintenance system.

**The four change types and their urgency**

| Change type | Example | Urgency | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pricing change | Competitor drops price by 30% | High — affects buying decision | Update within 48 hours of detection |
| New feature launch | Competitor ships a feature your page listed as a differentiator | High — reverses a key claim | Update within one week |
| Feature removal | Competitor removes a feature | Medium — less urgent, but removes a negative claim | Update at next review cycle |
| Acquisition or rebranding | Competitor is acquired, changes name | High — affects all references | Update within one week |
| Product shutdown | Competitor discontinues | Requires page restructure | Convert to "alternatives to [defunct product]" |

**The monitoring system**

Do not rely on manual checking. Set up automated alerts for every major competitor:

1. Google Alerts for competitor name + "pricing," competitor name + "feature," competitor name + "update."
2. G2 and Capterra review monitoring — new reviews often surface feature changes before the competitor announces them.
3. Competitor email newsletter subscription — pricing and feature changes are typically announced to existing subscribers first.
4. A simple quarterly calendar reminder to manually audit competitor pricing pages and feature documentation.

**The quarterly review checklist**

For every live competitor page, quarterly:

```
COMPETITOR PAGE QUARTERLY REVIEW
===================================
Page URL: [   ]
Review date: [   ]
Reviewer: [   ]

[ ] Competitor pricing verified against current pricing page (date: ___)
[ ] All features in comparison table verified against current documentation
[ ] "Where competitor wins" section still accurate
[ ] "Where competitor wins" section — any new advantages to add?
[ ] G2 and Capterra review trends — any new pattern in complaints or praise?
[ ] Competitor's domain authority / ranking position — still relevant comparison?
[ ] Any legal or trademark issue reported? (check inbox for C&D letters)
[ ] Customer quotes still accurate — any newer, more specific quotes available?
[ ] Internal links still live and landing on correct pages?
[ ] "Last updated" date updated on the page? [ ] Yes
[ ] Submitted for recrawl via Search Console? [ ] Yes
```

**The ownership model**

Competitor pages cannot be orphaned. Every page has a named owner responsible for monitoring and quarterly reviews. The ownership should sit with someone who has visibility into competitive intelligence — typically the product marketing manager or the SEO lead at the agency, not a content writer.

The brief must specify ownership from day one. A comparison page launched without a named owner will not be reviewed and will become inaccurate within six months.

---

**Common Mistake: Treating competitor pages as "publish and forget" content**

Every other content type in this course is relatively stable. A TOFU article about employee scheduling management stays relevant for years with minimal updates. A comparison page comparing your pricing against a competitor who changed their pricing model last quarter is actively misinforming buyers. Competitor pages are living assets. They require the same ongoing investment as any product that changes in a competitive market.

---

**Summary**

- Four change types require different urgency levels. Pricing changes and feature reversals need updates within one week. Everything else catches at quarterly review.
- Automated monitoring covers pricing changes, new features, and review patterns. Manual quarterly review covers everything else.
- Every competitor page has a named owner from launch. No owner means no maintenance.

## Lesson 7.6 — Building the Competitor Page Templates

Once the three page types are validated — one "vs" page, one "alternatives" page, one "tools like" page — the template enables production at scale. Each template is a brief format, not a content formula.

**The validation rule**

Build the first three competitor pages manually and fully. Get them ranking. Measure their conversion rates over 60 days. Only after confirming the template produces pages that rank and convert does production scale to the remaining competitor list.

A template that produces low-conversion pages at scale produces low conversion at scale. Validate before scaling.

**Template 1: "vs" comparison page**

```
[YOUR PRODUCT] VS [COMPETITOR] PAGE BRIEF
==========================================
URL: /compare/[your-product]-vs-[competitor]/
Title tag: [Your Product] vs [Competitor] ([Year]): Which Is Right for Your Team?
Meta description: Compare [Your Product] and [Competitor] on pricing, features, and
  fit. See which tool works better for [primary buyer type]. [Under 160 chars]
H1: [Your Product] vs [Competitor]: The Honest [Year] Comparison

SECTION 1 — OPENING (2 paragraphs)
  Para 1: What each product does in one sentence each. Neutral.
  Para 2: The core difference in one sentence. Why buyers compare these two.

SECTION 2 — QUICK VERDICT (above comparison table)
  [Your product] is better for: [specific buyer type]
  [Competitor] is better for: [specific buyer type]
  Format: two-column summary. 3 lines each.

SECTION 3 — SIDE-BY-SIDE TABLE
  Features to include (8–10 from sales "why we win/lose" notes):
    1.
    2.
    3.
  Pricing row: yes — source both from public pricing pages (date: ___)
  Data sources for competitor features: [G2 link / competitor docs URL / date]

SECTION 4 — WHERE [COMPETITOR] IS BETTER
  Genuine advantages (2–3): [List here — source from G2 reviews]
  Buyer type these serve: [Specify]

SECTION 5 — WHERE [YOUR PRODUCT] IS BETTER
  Genuine advantages (3–5): [List with specifics]
  Feature pages to link to per advantage: [URLs]

SECTION 6 — WHO SHOULD CHOOSE EACH
  [Competitor]: [Specific buyer context]
  [Your product]: [Specific buyer context]

SECTION 7 — CUSTOMER QUOTE
  Quote source: [Customer name, company — permission confirmed: Y/N]
  Quote must reference: [the specific comparison point that matters most]

SECTION 8 — FAQ (3–5 questions)
  Source from: sales call recordings, competitor G2 reviews
  Required questions:
    - Is [your product] cheaper than [competitor]?
    - Does [your product] integrate with [common integration]?
    - Can I migrate from [competitor] to [your product]?

CTA: [Exact text] → [Target URL]

DATA SOURCES:
  Competitor pricing: [URL + date]
  Competitor features: [G2 URL / competitor docs + date]
  Competitor weaknesses: [G2 "limitations" tag URL + date]

MAINTENANCE:
  Named owner: [   ]
```

**Template 2: "alternatives" page**

```

==========================================
URL: /alternatives/[competitor]-alternatives/

  Para 1: Why buyers look for [competitor] alternatives — specific documented pain points
    Source: [G2 "limitations" tag URL] [Reddit thread URL]
  Para 2: What to look for in an alternative — 3–4 criteria your product meets
  Para 3 (optional): Who this page is for

  All alternatives listed with: name, best for, pricing, and key differentiator
  Your product listed: [ ] First [ ] Second (based on genuine fit, not just placement)

  For each tool (4–5 alternatives including your product):
    What it is (1 sentence, neutral)
    Best for (specific buyer context)
    Key difference from [competitor]
    Pricing (public, dated)
    Where it falls short

YOUR PRODUCT SECTION (most detailed):
  Full feature coverage relevant to the buyer leaving [competitor]
  Screenshot: [ ] Required
  Customer quote from someone who switched from [competitor]: [ ] Required
  CTA: [Exact text] → [Target URL]

CONCLUSION:
  Decision matrix: one line per tool, matched to buyer type
  Final CTA

DATA SOURCES:
  [Competitor] pain points: [G2 URL + date / Reddit thread URL]
  Pricing for all alternatives: [URLs + dates]

MAINTENANCE:
  Named owner: [   ]
  Next review: [   ]
```

**Template 3: "tools like" page**

Shorter. Lower production investment. Lower conversion rate.

```
TOOLS LIKE [COMPETITOR] — PAGE BRIEF
=====================================
URL: /alternatives/tools-like-[competitor]/
Title tag: Tools Like [Competitor] in [Year]: [Number] Similar Apps Compared
H1: [Number] Tools Like [Competitor] Worth Considering

OPENING (1 paragraph):
  What [competitor] does well and why buyers explore similar options.

LIST SECTION (5–8 tools including your product):
  Each tool: name + one-sentence description + best for + pricing

YOUR PRODUCT ENTRY (slightly more detail than others):
  Name + two-sentence description + why it suits buyers exploring this category
  CTA: [Exact text] → [Target URL]

Maintenance: Annual review minimum (lower stakes than Types 1 and 2)
```

---

**Summary**

- Three templates, three brief formats. Validate manually before scaling.
- Each template specifies data sources, maintenance ownership, and next review date at the brief level — not as an afterthought after publishing.
- The "tools like" template is lighter because the intent stage is earlier and conversion rates are lower. Production investment should match conversion expectation.

## Module 7 Summary

Competitor and alternative pages are the highest-converting content category in SaaS SEO and the most maintenance-intensive. Three decisions determine whether the programme produces results or creates liability:

- **Build in priority order.** Type 1 "vs" pages for competitors you lose deals to. Type 2 "alternatives" pages for competitors whose dissatisfied users are your best prospects. Type 3 "tools like" pages after the first two are established and ranking. The graph method identifies which competitors to prioritise.
- **Honesty is the conversion mechanism.** Acknowledge genuine competitor strengths. Specify exactly which buyer type the competitor is better for. Introduce your product as the correct fit for the buyer who does not match that description. Promotional comparison pages convert below honest ones at every funnel stage.
- **Every page has a named owner and a review date at launch.** Competitor pages are not evergreen. They decay within months without active maintenance. Pricing changes, feature launches, and acquisitions require updates within days. The monitoring system and the quarterly review checklist are mandatory from day one.
