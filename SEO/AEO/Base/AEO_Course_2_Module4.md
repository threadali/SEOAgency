# AEO Course 2.0 — Module 4 Lecture Notes
## Content Architecture: Briefs, Hubs, and Clusters

**Module tagline:** Individual articles produce individual citations. Architecture produces citation authority.
**Who this is for:** Students who can identify content opportunities and understand what AI needs to cite them. This module covers how to structure content so that individual pieces and the system they sit within both produce citations.

---

## L4.1 — The Brief System

### Opening Hook

Most content briefs are a topic and a word count. Write 1,500 words about goldfish summer care. That brief produces a generic article. Generic articles do not get cited.

The AEO brief is a different document. It is the translation of everything collected in Modules 2 and 3 into a writing instruction. It takes forty minutes to complete. It cuts article writing time in half. And it is the difference between an article that gets cited in three weeks and one that sits unread for three months.

A journalist who walks into an assignment without knowing who their reader is, what the reader already knows, what they are worried about, and what angle their editor needs covered is guessing. The brief eliminates the guessing.

---

### Part 1: The Seven Fields

Every article brief has seven fields. Each one changes something specific about the article. None are optional.

**Field 1: Query**

The exact query the article targets. Not a topic. The specific query — written the way a user would actually type it or speak it.

Wrong: Goldfish care in summer
Right: How to keep goldfish alive in Lahore summer without air conditioning

The distinction matters because the query drives the opening sentence, the heading structure, and the FAQ questions. A topic-level brief produces a topic-level article. A query-level brief produces a citation-ready article.

One query per article. If you have two related queries that both need to be addressed, decide before writing whether they belong in one article or two. The Query FANMAP from Module 2 makes this decision.

---

**Field 2: ICP**

The exact persona for this specific article. Hyper-specific. Not "beginners" but: first-time goldfish buyer, Lahore apartment, young parent, child under five, no prior pet experience, no AC, limited budget.

The ICP determines the depth of explanation needed, the language register, the assumptions that can be made, and the constraints that need to be addressed. An article for an experienced hobbyist and an article for a first-time buyer on the same topic are completely different pieces.

If your FANMAP has three ICPs, most articles target one of them specifically. Only awareness-stage content that covers the broadest questions can serve multiple ICPs simultaneously.

---

**Field 3: Real Intent**

One sentence. What is this person actually trying to accomplish — not what the query literally says.

Surface query: How do I set up a goldfish tank?
Real intent: I want to know the minimum I need to do so my child's goldfish does not die before the end of the week.

The real intent goes into the opening paragraph as context. It tells the reader immediately that this article understands their actual situation — not just the topic. Content that matches real intent produces higher engagement signals (time on page, scroll depth, return visits) that feed back into AI citation probability.

---

**Field 4: Customer Language**

Eight to twelve phrases from your language bank for this topic. Sourced from Reddit, YouTube comments, reviews, and direct customer interaction — not from keyword tools or marketing language.

These phrases go into:
- H2 headings (match the way users actually phrase sub-questions)
- The opening paragraph (establish immediate relevance)
- FAQ questions (match the way customers will ask AI the same question)

AI embeds content semantically. Content using customer language produces closer semantic matches to user queries. This is a mechanical advantage, not a stylistic one.

---

**Field 5: Constraints**

All active constraints for this ICP on this specific query.

Budget, space, climate, equipment availability, family situation, experience level, time availability. Every constraint is both a reader context that makes the content more specific and a fan-out sub-query that AI will try to match.

An article that explicitly addresses six constraints produces closer matches to six constraint-specific sub-queries simultaneously. An article that addresses zero constraints matches only the base query.

The constraint list in the brief becomes a checklist for the Cover section of the article. If a constraint is not addressed in the article, that is a missing heading.

---

**Field 6: Competitor Gap**

The specific angle that no competitor is covering — sourced from the Topic Ownership Map and the after-reading gap research from Module 3.

One sentence: what will this article cover that nothing else currently answers?

This field has two purposes. It ensures that every article has genuine information gain built in from the start — not added as an afterthought. And it gives the writer a clear editorial angle that distinguishes the article from everything already published on the topic.

An article without a clear competitor gap is a generic article waiting to be written.

---

**Field 7: Context Coverage Targets**

A list of the specific fan-out sub-queries this article will cover. These are the headings.

Each sub-query becomes one H2 heading, written as a question. The list in the brief is the article's table of contents before the article exists. If a sub-query is on the list but the article does not cover it, the article has a structural gap.

This field also drives the article decision. If the context coverage targets span more than one ICP or more than one funnel stage, the article should be split into multiple pieces.

---

### Part 2: Brief-to-Draft Workflow

The brief produces the article structure before writing begins. Here is the workflow.

Step 1: Brief complete. All seven fields filled. Thirty to forty minutes.

Step 2: Article structure mapped. Using the brief, map: the opening sentence (from real intent + customer language), the TL;DR bullets (from the competitor gap and key constraints), the H2 headings (from context coverage targets, written as questions in customer language), and the FAQ questions (from the after-reading gap and constraint list).

Step 3: Identify information gain sources. Before writing, specify where the original data or specific insight will come from for each section. A heading without a clear information gain source should either be cut or moved to a different article that has stronger backing.

Step 4: Write. The structure is set. The information gain is identified. Writing is now execution, not discovery.

Step 5: Review against brief. After the first draft, check every field in the brief against the article. Is the real intent addressed in the opening? Is every constraint from field 5 addressed in the article? Does the article clearly answer the competitor gap in field 6? Does every context coverage target from field 7 have a heading?

---

### Part 3: Brief Across Business Types

The seven fields are universal. What changes across business types is what goes into each field.

For a local business: the query includes location. The ICP includes geographic context and local constraints. Customer language is in the local idiom. Constraints include climate, infrastructure, and local market-specific factors.

For a SaaS business: the query includes team size or company type. The ICP includes role and company stage. Customer language reflects how practitioners talk about the problem, not how marketers describe the solution. Constraints include budget per seat, technical team availability, and integration requirements.

For an e-commerce business: the query includes the purchase context. The ICP includes budget and buying situation. Customer language reflects what buyers say in reviews and comments. Constraints include local availability, pricing in local currency, and sizing or specification concerns.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Seven fields in the brief: query, ICP, real intent, customer language, constraints, competitor gap, context coverage targets
- Each field changes something specific about the article — none are optional
- The brief maps the article structure before writing begins
- Brief-to-draft workflow: fill brief → map structure → identify information gain sources → write → review against brief
- The brief eliminates guessing. No brief means guessing.

## L4.2 — The Answer Hub Model

### Opening Hook

A single article produces a single citation. When it is cited, one signal registers in AI's trust model for your brand on that one query.

An Answer Hub — a cluster of interconnected articles covering a full topic from multiple angles — produces a different kind of signal. AI encounters your brand across multiple sub-queries on the same topic, from the same domain, all pointing to each other. It stops treating you as a source for one answer and starts treating you as the authority on the topic.

That shift from source to authority is what produces citations on queries you never directly targeted.

---

### Part 1: Hub vs Cluster vs Standalone

These three terms describe different content structures. Understanding the difference prevents the most common content architecture mistake — building standalone articles and expecting cluster-level results.

**Standalone article:** One piece targeting one query. No related articles on the same topic cluster from the same domain. Citation probability depends entirely on that article's individual quality signals.

**Topic cluster:** A pillar article and a set of supporting articles, all internally linked. The pillar covers the broad topic at medium depth. The supporting articles each cover one sub-topic in depth. This was the HubSpot SEO model — it works for traditional search and it carries into AEO.

**Answer Hub:** An evolved topic cluster specifically designed for AI extraction. The difference from a standard topic cluster: every page in an Answer Hub is independently extractable. Each spoke article answers its specific question completely without requiring the reader (or AI) to visit the pillar or other spokes. The pillar provides navigation and context. The spokes provide citations.

The key structural distinction: in a topic cluster, the pillar is the most important page. In an Answer Hub, the spokes are the citation engines and the pillar is the connector.

---

### Part 2: Hub Architecture

**The pillar article:**
Covers the full topic at medium depth. Answers the broad question in a way that is satisfying but not exhaustive. Links to every spoke article. Is the right length to cover the full topic overview without trying to answer every sub-question in detail — typically 1,500 to 2,500 words.

Its job is not primarily to be cited. Its job is to establish topical authority for the domain on the broad topic and to serve as the navigation point that directs readers (and AI crawlers) to the specific answers they need.

**The spoke articles:**
Each spoke answers one specific sub-question from the pillar's fan-out. They are written as standalone pieces that make complete sense without the pillar. They link back to the pillar and to one or two related spokes where the connection is natural.

Spoke articles are where the citations happen. They are typically 800 to 1,500 words — focused, specific, citation-optimized.

**The FAQ hub page:**
An optional but high-value addition to the hub: a dedicated FAQ page that aggregates the most important questions across the full topic cluster into one page with FAQPage schema. This page exists specifically for AI extraction — it is the highest-density citation target in the hub.

---

### Part 3: Internal Linking Architecture

Internal links in an Answer Hub are not SEO crawl optimization. They are AI navigation signals. They tell AI that these articles belong together and come from the same topical authority.

**The rules:**

Every spoke links to the pillar. This is non-negotiable. The pillar needs to be the hub that AI can reach from every spoke.

The pillar links to every spoke. This allows AI crawlers traversing the pillar to reach every spoke article in one step.

Related spokes link to each other where the connection is natural. Natural means: a reader who finishes spoke A would genuinely benefit from reading spoke B. If the connection requires a stretch, the link does not belong.

No link goes in just to have a link. Every internal link should be placed at a point in the content where a human reader would genuinely want to explore further. Artificial link insertion is worse than no link — it signals to AI that the structure is forced.

**Anchor text:** Use descriptive anchor text that states what the linked article covers. Not "click here" or "read more" but "goldfish summer care in Lahore without AC" or "how to set up a goldfish tank on a budget."

---

### Part 4: Hub Building Sequence

Build the hub in a specific order. The sequence matters for citation development.

**Step 1: Build the FANMAP for the hub topic.**
Run a Query FANMAP on the broad topic. The sub-angles become your spoke article list. Group related sub-angles into single spokes where they share ICP and funnel stage. Separate them into distinct spokes where they serve different personas or different stages.

**Step 2: Write and publish the pillar first.**
The pillar needs to exist before spokes can link to it. Publish it first, even if it has placeholder internal links to spokes that are not yet written.

**Step 3: Publish spokes in priority order.**
Use the Phase 1 prioritization scoring from Module 2. O-grade spokes publish first. C-grade spokes follow. The internal links from the pillar update as each spoke publishes.

**Step 4: Add the FAQ hub page last.**
The FAQ page aggregates questions from across the hub. It is most complete and most accurate when all spokes are published. Build it after the spoke articles exist.

**Step 5: Monitor and update.**
Every six months, check which spokes are being cited and which are not. Underperforming spokes get a query analysis refresh — checking whether the brief was right, whether a better competitor has appeared, whether the information gain source is still valid.

---

### Part 5: Hub Size and Scope

A hub can be small (pillar + four spokes + FAQ page) or large (pillar + twelve spokes + multiple FAQ pages organized by sub-category). Size should be determined by the scope of the topic, not by a number target.

The minimum viable hub: one pillar + three spokes + one FAQ page. This is enough to establish topical authority signals on a narrow topic.

The problem with building too large too fast: quality distribution. A hub of twelve spokes where half are thin and poorly briefed produces worse results than a hub of five spokes where all five are comprehensive and citation-optimized. Build the minimum viable hub well, then extend it.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Answer Hub differs from topic cluster: spokes are the citation engines, every spoke is independently extractable
- Hub structure: pillar (navigation + broad authority) + spokes (citation engines) + FAQ hub (extraction target)
- Internal linking rules: every spoke to pillar, pillar to every spoke, related spokes to each other where natural
- Build in sequence: pillar first, O-grade spokes next, C-grade spokes after, FAQ hub last
- Minimum viable hub: pillar + three spokes + FAQ page. Build well, then extend

## L4.3 — Publishing Sequence Strategy

### Opening Hook

Publishing ten articles on the same topic in one week feels productive. It looks like commitment. It produces citations at approximately the same rate as publishing one article.

AI retrieval systems are not impressed by volume. They are influenced by behavioral signals that develop over time. A domain that publishes consistently, maintains its content, and accumulates engagement signals across a sustained period builds a different kind of authority than one that bursts content and goes quiet.

The publishing sequence is not a minor operational detail. It is a strategic decision that determines whether your content architecture produces compounding authority or isolated pages.

---

### Part 1: Why Cadence Matters to AI

AI retrieval systems do not simply index content and score it once. They observe patterns.

A domain that publishes two well-structured articles per week for three months signals a consistent, maintained content operation. A domain that publishes twenty articles in one week and then goes quiet signals a content farm.

The distinction matters for two reasons. First, freshness scoring in re-ranking models favors domains with consistent recent publishing activity over domains with sparse publishing patterns. Second, the behavioral signals that compound over time — backlinks generated by exposure, social sharing, community mentions — develop faster and more naturally from consistent publishing than from burst publishing.

The practical implication: two articles per week, every week, produces better results than eight articles one week and nothing for three weeks.

---

### Part 2: The Optimal Publishing Sequence

**Phase 1 sequence (weeks 1 through 8):**

Week 1: Publish the pillar article. Submit to Google and Bing Webmaster Tools. Add FAQPage schema and Article schema. Validate on Rich Results Test.

Week 2: Publish the first O-grade spoke. The highest priority score from your Phase 1 queue. Internal link from the pillar to the spoke and from the spoke back to the pillar.

Week 3: Publish the second O-grade spoke. Internal links added to both the pillar and the first spoke where relevant.

Weeks 4 through 8: One to two articles per week, continuing through the Phase 1 queue. Each article is connected to the existing hub before the next one publishes.

**Why this sequence:**
The pillar article starts accumulating signals first. By the time spokes publish, the pillar has had one to two weeks of crawling and indexing. Spokes that link to an already-indexed pillar get an immediate internal link equity signal that spokes published simultaneously with the pillar do not get.

**Phase 2 sequence (weeks 9 through 16):**

Shift from O-grade to C-grade articles. The same one to two per week cadence. The Phase 1 spokes are now accumulating citation signals — by week 8 or 9, Perplexity should be citing at least two to three of them.

Phase 2 articles link to relevant Phase 1 spokes, reinforcing the hub's internal link structure and extending the topical authority signal.

---

### Part 3: Refresh Cadence

Publishing new content is only half the sequence. Refreshing existing content is the other half.

Every published article should have a six-month refresh date added to the content calendar at the moment of publication. The refresh date is not a reminder to check the article — it is a scheduled edit session.

At the six-month refresh:

Check whether the article is still being cited. Run the target query in Perplexity and Google AI Overview. If the article is cited, the refresh is maintenance — update statistics, add any new angles that have emerged, check whether new competitors have appeared.

If the article is no longer cited, treat the refresh as a rewrite brief. Run the query analysis process from Module 3 again. Identify what has changed — whether a competitor published something better, whether the query intent has shifted, whether the information gain source is now out of date. Address the specific change, not the whole article.

If the article was never cited, the refresh brief needs to identify why. The most common causes: the OCS grade was wrong and the query is actually S-grade (outcompeted), the information gain was insufficient, or the brief identified the wrong intent. Each requires a different fix.

---

### Part 4: Platform-Specific Timing

Different platforms update their citation behavior at different speeds. Use this to calibrate expectations and feedback cycles.

Perplexity crawls frequently and updates citation behavior within days to two weeks of new content publishing. Check Perplexity first when assessing whether new content is performing.

Google AI Overview updates more slowly. Allow four to eight weeks after publishing before assessing Google AI Overview performance. Changes from content updates take a similar timeline.

ChatGPT Browse and Bing Copilot use Bing's index. Bing typically indexes faster than Google for new domains but can take one to three weeks for new content to appear in Browse results.

**The feedback loop:**
Publish → Check Perplexity at two weeks → If cited, evidence that content quality is sufficient → Check Google AI Overview at six weeks → If cited, Phase 1 success confirmed → Continue to Phase 2.

If Perplexity is citing at two weeks but Google AI Overview is not at six weeks, the issue is usually structural data (schema) or E-E-A-T signals, not content quality. If neither is citing, content quality or information gain is the issue.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Two articles per week beats eight articles in one week — consistency produces compounding signals, bursts produce diminishing returns
- Publish pillar first, then spokes in priority order, internal links updated with each new publication
- Phase 1 complete before Phase 2 begins — let O-grade signals accumulate before targeting C-grade
- Every article gets a six-month refresh date at publication
- Use Perplexity as two-week feedback signal, Google AI Overview as six-week confirmation signal

## L4.4 — Content Types and Their Citation Roles

### Opening Hook

Not all content is cited for the same reasons or at the same funnel stage. An awareness-stage educational article gets cited when someone asks an exploratory question. A VS page gets cited when someone asks a comparison question. A problem explainer gets cited when someone asks why something went wrong.

Writing the right content type for the wrong funnel stage is one of the most common AEO mistakes. You publish an educational guide and wonder why it does not appear when people are comparing options. You publish a comparison page and wonder why it is not cited for beginners' questions.

The content type should be determined by the query's funnel stage, not by personal preference or format habit.

---

### Part 1: The Five Core Content Types

**Type 1: Educational Guide**
*Funnel stage:* Pre-awareness and Awareness
*Citation trigger:* Exploratory "how do I" and "what is" queries at the awareness stage
*Structure:* Broad topic coverage with multiple sub-questions covered, strong TL;DR, comprehensive FAQ section
*Information gain requirement:* At least one original data point or specific contextual insight that generic guides do not contain
*Common mistake:* Writing an educational guide for a decision-stage query. A guide titled "Everything About Goldfish Care" will not be cited for "where to buy goldfish in Lahore" because the content type does not match the query intent.

---

**Type 2: Dedicated FANMAP Article (Micro-Answer)**
*Funnel stage:* Awareness and Consideration, specifically for O-grade constraint-specific queries
*Citation trigger:* Highly specific, constrained queries with thin existing coverage
*Structure:* Short, hyper-specific, single-question focus. Typically 800 to 1,200 words. Every section addresses the specific constraint. Standalone by design.
*Information gain requirement:* The article must address the specific constraint that makes the query O-grade. If the article removes the constraint from its coverage, it becomes generic and loses its information gain.
*Example:* "How to Keep Goldfish Alive During Lahore Load Shedding" — written specifically for the load shedding constraint, not as a general summer care guide with a mention of load shedding.

---

**Type 3: Comparison and VS Pages**
*Funnel stage:* Consideration and Evaluation
*Citation trigger:* "[Option A] vs [Option B]" and "best [X] for [Y]" queries where the user is comparing specific options
*Structure:* Clear comparison structure with a table, specific criteria for comparison, a clear recommendation at the end
*Information gain requirement:* The comparison must be based on something specific — actual testing, customer data, constraint-specific evaluation. A comparison that just lists features without a genuine evaluative perspective will not be cited.
*The recommendation rule:* AI strongly prefers comparison content that takes a position. "Fantail goldfish are better for small apartments than common goldfish because they produce less waste and require slightly less tank volume" is more citable than "both are good options depending on your needs."

---

**Type 4: Alternative and Anti-Audience Pages**
*Funnel stage:* Evaluation and Decision
*Citation trigger:* "[Competitor] alternative" and "best alternative to [X]" queries from buyers actively considering switching or comparing against an established option
*Structure:* Acknowledges the competitor's strengths honestly, identifies specific situations where an alternative is better, provides the alternative recommendation with specific reasoning
*The honesty principle:* Dishonest alternative pages that pretend the competitor has no strengths are not trusted by AI or by readers. An alternative page that says "Intercom is excellent for enterprise teams but over-engineered for a five-person startup — here is what works better at that scale" is more citable than one that attacks the competitor.
*Information gain requirement:* The alternative must be specifically better for a specific constraint or situation. Generic "we are better" claims produce zero information gain.

---

**Type 5: Problem Explainer**
*Funnel stage:* Pre-awareness
*Citation trigger:* "Why is my X doing Y" queries where the user does not yet know what caused the problem or what to do about it
*Structure:* Opens by validating the problem and naming it clearly. Explains the mechanism (why it happens). Then transitions to what to do about it.
*The transition rule:* Problem explainers that stop at explaining the problem leave the reader stranded. The most valuable problem explainers connect the diagnosis to the solution — ideally to a solution the brand provides or an article the brand has written.
*Information gain requirement:* The specific cause-and-effect mechanism behind the problem. Generic advice like "goldfish die due to poor water quality" is not a mechanism. "Goldfish die in the first week most often due to a nitrogen spike from the new tank syndrome — an uncycled tank has no beneficial bacteria to process ammonia from fish waste" is a mechanism. That specificity is what gets cited.

---

### Part 2: Content Type Selection Matrix

| Funnel stage | Query pattern | Content type |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-awareness | Why is my X doing Y? | Problem explainer |
| Awareness | How do I X? What is Y? | Educational guide or dedicated micro-answer |
| Consideration | Best X for Y, top options | Educational guide or VS page |
| Evaluation | X vs Y, X alternative | VS page or alternative page |
| Decision | Where to buy X, X reviews, can X do Y | Landing page or FAQ-heavy product page |

---

### Part 3: Content Type Mistakes That Hurt Citation Rates

**Wrong type for the funnel stage:** The most common mistake. A decision-stage page targeting an awareness-stage query. The query intent does not match the content format. AI recognizes the mismatch and does not cite.

**Educational guide where a micro-answer is needed:** A 3,000-word comprehensive guide targeting a specific O-grade constraint query. AI extracts the relevant paragraph — but a 900-word dedicated micro-answer that covers only that constraint would have a higher extraction score because every paragraph is relevant to the specific query.

**VS page without a recommendation:** Comparison content that presents both options neutrally and ends with "it depends on your needs." AI cannot extract a citable answer from a non-answer. Take a position.

**Alternative page that attacks the competitor:** Low trust, low citation probability. The alternative page that honestly acknowledges the competitor's strengths while positioning the alternative for a specific context earns more trust from both AI and readers.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Five core content types, each with a specific funnel stage and citation trigger
- Type is determined by query intent, not by personal preference
- Comparison and alternative pages must take a clear position — neutral non-answers do not get cited
- Problem explainers must include the mechanism, not just the problem
- Micro-answer articles should stay hyper-specific — generalization defeats their citation purpose

## L4.5 — Content Decay and the Refresh Cycle

### Opening Hook

Most AEO guides teach you how to get cited. Almost none teach you how to stay cited.

A piece of content that earns a citation does not hold it indefinitely. The world moves. Competitors improve. AI systems update their retrieval indexes. A well-cited article from eight months ago can quietly slip out of AI responses without anyone noticing — until the client reports that AI referral traffic has dropped and they cannot explain why.

Content decay is predictable, measurable, and reversible if caught early. Ignored for twelve to eighteen months, it becomes expensive to fix.

---

### Part 1: The Three Triggers of Citation Loss

**Trigger 1: The content becomes outdated**

Statistics age. Product recommendations change. Regulations update. Pricing shifts. An article that cited a price range from 2023 in a market that has since changed is now providing inaccurate information. AI systems assign lower confidence to content containing claims that conflict with more recently published information from the same retrieval set.

Outdated content is the most common and most fixable cause of citation loss. The solution is straightforward: update the specific outdated claims, refresh the statistics, update the dateModified in the Article schema. A genuinely refreshed article typically recovers citations within four to six weeks.

**Trigger 2: A competitor publishes something better**

A competitor who was absent from your topic six months ago has published a comprehensive article that addresses three angles your article does not. AI begins citing them on those sub-queries and gradually shifts to citing them as the primary source on the broader query.

This trigger is identifiable: run the query in Perplexity and Google AI Overview and check which source appears. If a new competitor appears on queries where you were previously cited, read their article. Find the specific improvement they made. Address it.

The competitor displacement recovery is not a full rewrite. It is a targeted update that addresses the specific gap the competitor filled. Adding a new H2 section, updating a FAQ, or improving a specific claim that the competitor addressed more thoroughly than you did.

**Trigger 3: The query landscape shifts**

A new sub-query has emerged that your article does not cover. A new constraint has become relevant to your ICP that did not exist when you wrote the article. A platform update, a cultural event, or a market change has produced new questions around your topic that post-date your article.

This trigger is the hardest to catch without monitoring. The symptom: your article is still being cited for some sub-queries but not for others, and the uncited sub-queries are recent or emerging. The fix: run the FANMAP for the article topic again. New sub-queries that appear in current fan-out output and are not covered in your article become new headings.

---

### Part 2: The Six-Month Audit

Every article should be reviewed six months after publishing. The audit has five checks:

**Check 1: Citation status**
Run the target query in Perplexity, Google AI Overview, and ChatGPT Browse. Is the article still being cited? If yes, which specific claims or sections are being extracted? If no, move to the diagnostic process.

**Check 2: Competitor check**
Are any new competitors appearing for this query that were absent at the time of publishing? If yes, read their content and identify the specific improvement they made.

**Check 3: Statistics and claims review**
Are all statistics still current? Have any specific claims been superseded by newer published data? Any claim that cites data more than 18 months old should be verified and updated.

**Check 4: Fan-out coverage check**
Run the query in ChatGPT: "What sub-questions would someone asking [query] have that most articles fail to answer?" Compare the output to your article's heading structure. Any new sub-question not covered is a new section to add.

**Check 5: Schema validation**
Re-run the article URL through Google Rich Results Test. Schema breaks without warning when CMS updates or template changes occur. A broken FAQPage schema is invisible to the site owner but immediately affects citation extraction.

---

### Part 3: Update vs Republish Decision

Not every refresh requires the same approach. The decision between updating the existing article and republishing as a new piece depends on what has changed.

**Update the existing article when:**
- Statistics and specific claims need refreshing but the core topic, ICP, and intent remain valid
- One or two new sub-questions need to be added as new headings
- A competitor has appeared and the fix requires adding a specific section or improving a specific FAQ

Updating preserves the URL's existing citation history and trust signals. An established URL with existing citations has authority that a new URL does not have. Always prefer updating over republishing when the core content is still valid.

**Republish as a new piece when:**
- The original brief was wrong — the query intent, ICP, or funnel stage was misidentified and the article is structurally misaligned
- The topic has changed so fundamentally that fewer than 40% of the original content is still applicable
- The original URL carries negative signals from thin content, schema violations, or manual actions that cannot be reversed

Republishing resets the citation clock. All existing authority attached to the old URL is lost. The new URL starts from zero. This cost is only worth paying when the existing URL's content is genuinely irreparable.

---

### Part 4: The Decay Prevention Mindset

The businesses that sustain citation authority over years are the ones that treat content as an asset requiring maintenance, not a campaign requiring deployment.

Every article published is a long-term commitment. It needs a publishing date, a first review date (two weeks, for Perplexity citation check), a Orbit Map check date (six weeks, for Google AI Overview), and a full audit date (six months).

Build these dates into whatever content calendar system the team uses. A content team that creates articles without scheduling their maintenance is publishing perishable assets and treating them as permanent ones.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Three triggers of citation loss: content becomes outdated, competitor improves, query landscape shifts
- Six-month audit: five checks — citation status, competitors, statistics, fan-out coverage, schema validation
- Update existing URLs unless the original brief was structurally wrong or the topic has changed fundamentally
- Every article needs four scheduled dates at publication: two-week check, six-week check, six-month audit
- Content is an asset requiring maintenance — treat it accordingly

## Lab 2: Build a Complete Answer Hub

**Objective:** Produce a fully structured Answer Hub for one topic, ready for article writing.

**Deliverable:** A document containing:
- The hub topic and pillar article brief (all seven fields completed)
- Six spoke article titles with the query each targets and the OCS grade of each query
- Brief for each spoke article (all seven fields)
- Internal linking map: which articles link to which and with what anchor text
- FAQ hub page question list (minimum fifteen questions across the full hub topic)
- Publishing sequence calendar: which article publishes in which week and why

**Time:** Four to six hours.

**Quality check before submitting:**
- Does every spoke article cover a distinct sub-query from the pillar's fan-out?
- Is every spoke independently extractable — can it be read and understood without the pillar?
- Does every brief have a genuine competitor gap in field 6?
- Does the internal linking map have the pillar linking to every spoke and every spoke linking back to the pillar?
- Does the publishing sequence follow the Phase 1 sequence: pillar first, O-grade spokes next, C-grade spokes after?

## Module 4 Closing

Content architecture is what separates a collection of articles from a citation system.

The brief ensures every article is built from intelligence, not guesswork. The Answer Hub structure produces topical authority that compounds across spokes. The publishing sequence builds signals over time rather than creating a burst that diminishes. The right content type for the right funnel stage ensures that content lands where it will be cited. And the refresh cycle keeps the architecture alive and performing long after the initial publication energy fades.
