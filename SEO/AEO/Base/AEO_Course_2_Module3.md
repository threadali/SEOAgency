# AEO Course 2.0 — Module 3 Lecture Notes
## Competitor Intelligence System

**Module tagline:** Knowing who your competitors are is step one. Knowing exactly what they missed is what you actually need.
**Who this is for:** Students who can identify AI competitors and run basic OCS grading. This module goes into the analysis layer — how to extract intelligence from competitors that feeds directly into article briefs.

---

## L3.1 — The Three Competitor Types Revisited: Confirming What You Are Facing

### Opening Hook

In v1.0 you learned the three competitor types. Real AI Competitors, Active Competitors, Lucky Competitors. The classification was based on how often and how consistently they appeared in AI responses.

That classification still holds. But knowing the type is not the same as knowing how to respond to it. In v2.0 we go one level deeper: how do you confirm which type you are dealing with using live checks, and what does that confirmation change about your strategy?

---

### Part 1: The Confirmation Test

Orbit Map identification gives you a preliminary classification. Confirmation requires three additional checks.

**Check 1: Cross-query consistency**
Run five to eight related queries in each platform. Count how many times the competitor appears.

A Real AI Competitor appears on four or more of eight queries, across at least two platforms consistently. They are not appearing on one strong topic — they are appearing everywhere in the space.

An Active Competitor appears on two or three of eight queries, typically on specific angles where they have invested heavily. Strong in certain subtopics. Absent on others.

A Lucky Competitor appears on one or two queries, typically the same ones every time. If you run variations of that query, they disappear. Their citation is tied to one piece, not to broad topical authority.

**Check 2: Content operation check**
Visit the competitor's website and check three things.

Publishing frequency: Are they publishing consistently? A Real AI Competitor typically publishes multiple pieces per month with an active content team. An Active Competitor has irregular but growing output. A Lucky Competitor typically has low publishing activity — the cited piece is often surrounded by thin or outdated content.

Content depth: Are their articles comprehensive or surface-level? Real AI Competitors produce comprehensive, well-structured content. Lucky Competitors often have one well-performing piece surrounded by thin content.

Last updated dates: Are they maintaining their content? Stale dateModified signals on a competitor's cited articles are an opening. They are being cited for content they are not maintaining. You can displace them with a current, better-maintained version.

**Check 3: Brand signal audit**
Search the competitor brand name in ChatGPT and ask: what is this brand known for in this topic area?

A Real AI Competitor has a strong, specific, consistent brand description. AI knows exactly what they do and cites them confidently.

An Active Competitor gets a partial description — AI knows them for one or two angles but not for the whole space.

A Lucky Competitor either gets no description or a generic one. AI is citing their content but has not formed strong entity recognition around the brand.

---

### Part 2: What Type Confirmation Changes About Your Strategy

**If facing a Real AI Competitor:**
Do not compete head-to-head on their strongest topics. Your time is better spent finding the sub-topics they have not covered, the constraint-specific angles they sacrifice for broad reach, and the local or context-specific gaps their international audience orientation creates.

Real AI Competitors are typically broad. They have to be — their audience is global. Every local or niche constraint they ignore is your content opportunity.

**If facing an Active Competitor:**
These are your most productive competitive targets. They have a strategy that is partially working, which means you can study what they are doing right, learn from their structure and depth, and then find the specific angles they have not reached.

Active Competitors often dominate one or two funnel stages and have thin coverage at others. If they are strong at awareness and weak at evaluation, the evaluation stage is yours.

**If facing a Lucky Competitor:**
Move fast. Identify the exact article producing their citation. Analyze it: what does it cover, what does it miss, what is outdated, what constraints is it ignoring?

Write a better version. Publish it. Lucky Competitors have nothing behind their citation to defend it. Most are displaced within four to eight weeks by a more complete, more current, better-structured article on the same topic.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Cross-query consistency, content operation check, and brand signal audit confirm the competitor type
- Real AI Competitors are broad — find the niche and local angles they cannot cover
- Active Competitors have partial strategies — find their weak funnel stage and own it
- Lucky Competitors have fragile citations — move fast and publish a better version

## L3.2 — The One-Time Competitor Study

### Opening Hook

Most people do competitor research continuously. They check competitors when they are about to write a specific article, which means they are always starting from scratch and never building a cumulative picture.

The one-time competitor study is done once, thoroughly, for all identified competitors. It produces a Topic Ownership Map and a base context that every subsequent article brief draws from. You do it once. You reference it always. You update it when a competitor changes significantly.

---

### Part 1: The Four Questions

Run these four questions for every competitor in your Orbit Map. Core Orbit competitors get the deepest treatment. Mid Orbit get a full pass. Outer Orbit / Lucky Competitors get a quick pass — enough to identify the one angle driving their citation.

**Question 1: What is their primary USP?**

You are looking for the specific angle they are known for — in AI responses, not in their own marketing materials.

Check both:

ChatGPT prompt:
```
Analyze [Competitor URL or Brand Name].
What is their primary USP in [niche]?
What are they most known for in AI responses?
```

Then manually run five topic queries in Perplexity and Google AI Overview and note which angle they are consistently associated with in the AI-generated answers.

The manual check is important because the ChatGPT prompt tells you what AI knows about the brand's content. The manual check tells you what AI actually uses the brand for. These are sometimes different.

**Question 2: Which topics do they consistently cover?**

ChatGPT prompt:
```
List the top 10 topics that [Competitor] consistently covers.
What subject areas do they dominate in search and AI responses?
```

Then check their website's content archive or blog. Group their articles into topic categories. The categories with five or more articles represent their topical focus areas. These are the areas where they have the most citation authority.

Note both: what they cover deeply (five or more articles) and what they cover superficially (one or two thin articles). Superficial coverage is exploitable even within their own strong topics.

**Question 3: Which angles do they own in AI responses?**

This question is answered manually, not with a prompt.

Run ten to fifteen topic queries across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overview. Every time the competitor appears, note the exact angle their content addresses. After fifteen queries, a pattern becomes clear.

Competitor A might appear every time a query involves tank setup or equipment. They own the equipment angle. Competitor B might appear every time a query involves fish health and disease. They own the care and health angle.

These owned angles are their strongest positions. You can challenge them there, but it requires substantially better content. More often, noting these owned angles shows you what is already claimed and redirects your attention to what is not.

**Question 4: Which angles are they missing?**

This is the most important question and requires active thinking rather than a prompt.

For each competitor, write down what their content consistently does not address. Use these filters:

- Do they have local or geographic specificity? If not, every local variant is a gap.
- Do they address budget constraints in local currency? If not, every budget-specific variant is a gap.
- Do they address constraint-specific situations (no equipment, limited space, extreme climate)? If not, every constraint combination is a gap.
- Do they address the full funnel? If their content is strong at awareness but thin at evaluation, the evaluation stage is open.
- Do they address negative angles — worst options, common mistakes, what not to do? Most content is positive and promotional. Negative angles are underrepresented almost everywhere.

---

### Part 2: Building the Topic Ownership Map

Once all competitors have been through the four questions, build the Topic Ownership Map. This is a grid that shows, at a glance, which topics are owned, which are contested, and which are completely unclaimed.

**Structure:**

| Topic | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C | Gap? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Topic 1 | Owns | Covers | Absent | No |
| Topic 2 | Absent | Absent | Absent | YES |
| Topic 3 | Covers | Covers | Covers | No |

"Owns" means this competitor is the dominant cited source for this topic.
"Covers" means they have content on it but are not the dominant cited source.
"Absent" means they have nothing on this topic.

**Gap identification:** Any row where all three competitors are "Absent" is an uncontested gap. Any row where all three are "Covers" but none "Owns" is a contested gap — an opportunity for a better piece to take the dominant position.

---

### Part 3: Setting the Base Context

After completing the study for all competitors, set a base context document. This is a short reference used in every subsequent article brief.

It contains three things:

Your own USPs: the two or three specific things that differentiate your business from every competitor in the map. These get naturally woven into every article — not as promotion, but as genuine contextual differentiators.

Owned angles: the topics from the map where you have already published and are being cited. These are areas where you have built authority and can reference in new content as related resources.

Exclusive gaps: the starred rows from the Topic Ownership Map — topics where all competitors are absent and you have original information to provide. These are your Phase 1 content targets.

---

### Part 4: When to Update the Competitor Study

The full four-question study should be repeated every six months. Competitors move. Active Competitors sometimes become Real AI Competitors faster than expected. Lucky Competitors occasionally build real strategies.

The Topic Ownership Map should be spot-checked monthly — specifically for your Phase 1 topics. If a previously absent competitor has published on one of your exclusive gaps, you need to know so you can respond before they establish citation authority.

---

### Lesson Summary

- One-time competitor study runs four questions for every identified competitor
- The output is a Topic Ownership Map showing owned, contested, and gap topics
- Gaps where all competitors are absent are your exclusive territory — Phase 1 targets
- Set a base context document after completing the study — it feeds into every article brief
- Full study every six months, spot-check monthly on your Phase 1 topics

## L3.3 — Query-Level Analysis: The Four Research Layers

### Opening Hook

The one-time competitor study tells you what topics are available. Query-level analysis tells you how to win them.

Before writing any article, you need four things that no keyword tool or AI prompt can give you: the exact language your customers use when describing their problem, what they are actually trying to accomplish (which is different from what the query says), the specific constraints they are working within, and what their experience level with existing content has been.

These four things come from four research layers. They are collected before writing begins. They feed directly into the article brief.

---

### Layer 1: Authentic Customer Language

**What it is:** The exact phrases customers use when describing their problem or situation — in their own words, not in marketing language.

**Why it matters mechanically:** AI converts queries into semantic embeddings and matches them to content embeddings. Content that uses the same natural language as user queries produces closer embedding matches. Content written in marketing language produces weaker matches even when covering the same topic.

"Premium aquatic livestock solutions for discerning pet enthusiasts" and "my goldfish keeps dying what am I doing wrong" are about the same topic. Only one of them will be cited in response to the user's query.

**Where to find it:**

Reddit: Search site:reddit.com [topic] help — then site:reddit.com [topic] confused — then site:reddit.com [topic] dying. Read the post titles and the first lines of high-upvote posts. These are unfiltered, unmarketed descriptions of real problems.

YouTube comments: On competitor video content, search for comments that describe the viewer's situation. These are written the moment someone is experiencing the problem and looking for help.

Google reviews of competitors: Reviews written by actual customers about their experience contain the exact language they used when searching. A review that says "I was a complete beginner and had no idea where to start" contains the search language of a first-time buyer.

Facebook groups: Search for your topic in relevant groups. Posts asking for help are written in customer language.

**What to collect:** Build a language bank — a running list of the exact phrases customers use to describe their situation. Specific words, specific metaphors, specific ways of framing the problem.

This language goes directly into H2 headings, opening sentences, and FAQ questions. It does not replace expert language — but it wraps around it. The expert answer, delivered in the reader's own language.

---

### Layer 2: Real Customer Intent

**What it is:** What the customer is actually trying to accomplish — which is often different from what the query literally says.

**Why it matters:** Surface-intent content answers the literal question. Real-intent content answers the underlying need. AI systems are increasingly good at detecting intent mismatch — when a page technically addresses a query but does not satisfy the underlying need.

**Surface vs real intent examples:**

"Best goldfish for beginners" — surface intent: want a species recommendation. Real intent: want a fish that will not die quickly because they have already had one die and it was emotionally difficult.

"Goldfish tank setup" — surface intent: want setup instructions. Real intent: want to know the minimum viable setup that will keep the fish alive without expensive equipment they cannot afford.

"Customer support software comparison" — surface intent: want a feature comparison table. Real intent: want to know which tool their team will actually adopt and use without requiring IT involvement.

**How to find it:**

Reddit threads with high upvote counts often reveal real intent in the comments. A post with a surface-level question that accumulates responses showing what the poster was actually worried about exposes the gap between what people type and what they need.

The trigger identified in the ICP model also reveals real intent. A customer whose fish just died is not asking an exploratory question — they are asking a failure-prevention question. The content that addresses that emotional context alongside the practical information will outperform content that provides the same practical information without it.

---

### Layer 3: Constraint Extraction

**What it is:** The specific limitations and conditions under which the customer needs to apply the information.

**Why it matters for AEO:** Constraints are where most existing content fails. Generic advice is written for ideal conditions. Real buyers rarely have ideal conditions. A customer in Lahore with a small apartment, no AC, a child in the house, a 2000 PKR budget, and no prior experience has six active constraints that modify every piece of advice they receive.

Each constraint is also a fan-out sub-query. Content that explicitly addresses a constraint will be extracted by AI when that constraint appears in a user's query. Content that ignores constraints will not.

**How to extract them:**

ChatGPT prompt for constraint generation:
```
List all possible constraints for a person asking [query] in Pakistan.
Consider: budget, space, climate, family situation, experience level,
equipment availability, time availability, and any other relevant factors.
```

Then filter the output through your ICP definition. The constraints that match your ICP are the ones that belong in your content.

**Building the constraint bank:**

For each target topic, document every constraint your ICP is likely to have. This constraint bank serves two purposes. It generates additional fan-out headings for the article — each constraint becomes a section or a FAQ entry. And it provides the context layer that makes your content uncopyable by competitors who are writing from generic premises.

An article that opens with: "If you are keeping goldfish in a Lahore apartment without air conditioning, on a budget under 2,000 rupees, with a small child in the house — this guide is specifically for you" will extract for every one of those constraint sub-queries because the language is in the content.

---

### Layer 4: Gap Identification

**What it is:** The specific questions a customer still has after reading all the existing content on a topic.

**Why it matters:** This is where information gain actually comes from in practice. Not from covering topics others cover — from answering questions others raise but do not resolve. Every article that explains tank setup but does not address what to do when the power goes out during a Lahore summer has created a gap. You fill it.

**How to find gaps:**

Reddit gap searches:
```
site:reddit.com "[topic]" "read" "still confused"
site:reddit.com "[topic]" "article" "doesn't answer"
site:reddit.com "[topic]" "nobody talks about"
```

These searches find the specific frustrations of people who have already consumed existing content and found it insufficient. A Reddit post with forty upvotes saying "read five articles on goldfish care, none mention what to do in Lahore heat without AC" is documented proof of a gap with verified demand.

The after-reading gap prompt:
```
What questions would someone still have after reading the top five articles 
about [topic]? What do most articles assume that the reader in [specific 
context] might not have access to or understand?
```

This prompt, run against your topic with your ICP context specified, generates a list of gaps. Not all will be valid — filter them against your own knowledge of what actually matters to your customer. The ones that are both real and unanswered in existing content are your highest-priority information gain opportunities.

---

### Part 2: Assembling the Query Analysis Output

After running all four layers for a target query, you have everything needed for the article brief.

The query analysis output:

- Customer language bank: 8 to 12 phrases in the customer's own words
- Real intent statement: one sentence describing what the customer is actually trying to accomplish
- Constraint list: all active constraints for the target ICP on this specific query
- Gap list: 3 to 5 specific questions not answered by existing content

This output feeds directly into the brief template from Module 4.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Four research layers before every article: customer language, real intent, constraints, gaps
- Customer language produces closer semantic embedding matches — use it in headings and FAQ questions
- Real intent is different from surface intent — content that addresses both outperforms content addressing only one
- Constraints generate additional fan-out headings and make content specific enough to be uncopyable
- Gap identification is how you find information gain — look for what existing content raises but does not resolve

## L3.4 — The After-Reading Gap

### Opening Hook

Every competitor who publishes a good article on your target topic is doing you a service.

They are attracting readers who finish the article and still have unanswered questions. Those readers go back to the search bar and type again. Sometimes the second query contains a constraint the first article ignored. Sometimes it is a follow-up question the article implied but did not answer. Sometimes it is a specific situation the reader is in that the article covered only in general terms.

The after-reading gap is that second query. It is the most powerful content opportunity available because the competitor has done the hard work of creating demand. You do the work of satisfying it.

---

### Part 1: Why After-Reading Gaps Produce the Fastest Citations

A regular O-grade topic is a vacuum. Nothing exists. You publish, and AI cites you because you are the only option.

An after-reading gap topic is even better than a vacuum. The competitor's content has been cited. Readers have visited it. Many have found it insufficient. The demand for a better answer is documented in Reddit threads, YouTube comments, and follow-up searches.

When you publish a piece that specifically addresses the gap left by a competitor's well-cited article, three things happen:

First, AI has a reason to cite you specifically rather than the competitor — because your content addresses the sub-query the competitor's content created but did not satisfy.

Second, readers who have been frustrated by the competitor's content and are searching for the missing piece find yours and engage deeply. High time-on-page and low bounce rate send positive signals.

Third, the competitor's citation helps yours. Because the competitor is already cited, their article appears in AI responses and sends readers who need more depth toward the topic area where you are the specialist.

---

### Part 2: How to Find After-Reading Gaps Systematically

**Method 1: Read the competitor's article as your ICP**

Take your ICP definition. Read the competitor's best article on your target topic through the eyes of that exact customer. Write down every question you still have after finishing the article.

Do not read it as a content professional looking for structural gaps. Read it as a first-time goldfish buyer in Lahore with a 2,000 rupee budget who just got home with their fish and is trying to figure out what to do next. Where does the article leave you stranded?

**Method 2: Search for reader frustrations**

The Reddit search pattern from Layer 4 in L3.3:
```
site:reddit.com "[competitor brand or article title topic]" "still confused"
site:reddit.com "[topic]" "read about" OR "read the article" "but"
```

The word "but" after a reference to reading something is almost always followed by a gap statement. "I read the article on goldfish care but it did not say anything about what to do when the power goes out" is an explicit after-reading gap with documented demand.

**Method 3: Analyze the competitor's comment sections**

Articles with comment sections show exactly what readers still needed after reading. Sort comments by upvotes or most liked. The highest-upvoted questions in a competitor's comment section are the questions the article raised but did not answer.

**Method 4: Use the AI interrogation technique**

Ask AI directly:
```
I read the top articles about [topic]. What important questions did they 
likely not answer well? What information is typically missing from content 
about [topic] for someone in [specific situation]?
```

This works because AI has processed most of the major content on any topic and has registered which sub-queries consistently go unanswered. It will often tell you its own gaps in the topic area if asked correctly.

---

### Part 3: Turning an After-Reading Gap Into an Article

An after-reading gap article has a specific structure different from a standard AQCE article.

**The opening acknowledges the journey:**
The reader has already read something. They know the basics. Do not re-explain what the competitor already explained. Start where the competitor left off. The opening line might be: "Most goldfish care guides cover tank size and feeding schedules. None of them explain what to do when the power goes out in Lahore summer and your fish tank overheats."

That opening sentence does two things. It tells the reader immediately that this article is specifically for their situation — the one the other article missed. And it signals to AI that this article covers a sub-query that existing content on the topic does not address.

**The content is exclusively gap-focused:**
Do not re-cover what the competitor covered. You are not writing a comprehensive guide — you are writing the specific answer to the specific question they left unanswered. This makes the article shorter, more specific, and more extractable than a general comprehensive guide.

**The FAQ section covers the specific after-reading questions:**
Not the general topic questions the competitor already answered. The specific questions that come after reading their article. These are the highest-priority FAQ entries because they match exactly the second-query searches that after-reading gap demand generates.

**Link to the comprehensive guide:**
If appropriate, link to a comprehensive guide on the topic — either your own or the competitor's. This signals that your article is a supplement, not a replacement. AI handles supplementary content well — it can cite the comprehensive guide for broad questions and your gap article for the specific sub-query.

---

### Part 4: After-Reading Gaps Across Business Types

The after-reading gap pattern shows up differently across business types but follows the same logic everywhere.

**Local business:** The comprehensive guide exists. It does not mention the local context. Your after-reading gap article is the local-context version of everything the guide implied but could not answer for a local audience.

**E-commerce:** The product review exists. It does not mention real-world usage in specific conditions — local climate, budget constraints, specific use cases. Your after-reading gap article covers what the review reader was wondering about after they read it.

**SaaS:** The comparison article exists. It does not address implementation reality — how long it actually takes to set up, what the learning curve is for a non-technical team, what goes wrong in the first month. Your after-reading gap article covers what the comparison-reader was thinking about but could not find.

**Education:** The course overview exists. It does not address what learning looks like for someone from a specific background — a Pakistani freelancer, a working professional with four hours per week, someone without a laptop. Your after-reading gap article is the context-specific version of the review they already read.

---

### Part 5: After-Reading Gaps as a Compounding Strategy

The businesses that make after-reading gap content a systematic part of their content strategy rather than an occasional tactic build a specific kind of authority over time.

Each gap article creates a dependency relationship with the competitor's content. When AI processes a topic area and finds the competitor's article and your gap article, it begins treating them as a pair. The competitor answers the general question. You answer the specific follow-up. Over time AI learns to cite you specifically when the follow-up is needed — without you having to displace the competitor on their main article.

This is a different path to authority than direct displacement. Instead of fighting the competitor for their citation, you extend it. You become the specialist the generalist's audience turns to when they need more.

---

### Lesson Summary

- After-reading gaps are questions the competitor's content creates but does not answer — the most powerful information gain source
- Find them through ICP-perspective reading, Reddit frustration searches, comment section analysis, and AI interrogation
- After-reading gap articles start where competitors stopped — do not re-cover what was already covered
- FAQ entries in gap articles should match second-query searches, not first-query questions
- Systematic after-reading gap content builds a dependency relationship with competitor citations over time

---

## Module 3 Closing

Competitor intelligence is not about watching what competitors do. It is about extracting from their work the specific opportunities they have left behind.

The one-time competitor study maps the territory — which topics are owned, which are contested, which are empty. The query-level analysis provides the customer intelligence that makes your content specific enough to be uncopyable. The after-reading gap identifies the highest-leverage content type available: the answer to the question the competitor's work is already generating demand for.

Module 4 moves into content architecture — how to structure an Answer Hub, how to build the brief, and how publishing sequence and internal linking create the topical authority signal that makes individual articles perform better than they would in isolation.
