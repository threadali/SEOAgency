# AEO Course 2.0 — Module 2 Lecture Notes
## Research Architecture: FANMAP v2.0

**Module tagline:** Most content plans are built on assumptions. This module replaces assumptions with a system.
**Who this is for:** Students who ran FANMAP in v1.0 and want the full architecture — all six types, the Orbit Map grid, all twelve layers, and the prioritization logic that turns raw output into a phased content calendar.

---

## L2.1 — FANMAP v2.0 Overview: What Changed and Why

### Opening Hook

In v1.0, FANMAP was taught as one thing: a topic discovery tool. You built a map of queries, graded them OCS, and used the O-grade ones as your content targets.

That was correct. But it was incomplete.

The problem practitioners ran into: they would build a FANMAP, get 80 or 100 topics, start writing, and three months later realize they had written articles for queries nobody was actually searching in their specific context. Or they had gone after C and S grade topics because they looked important, and their O-grade opportunities — the fast ones — were untouched.

v2.0 fixes this with three changes.

**Change 1: FANMAP has two separate use cases with two separate outputs.**
Business FANMAP maps the full topic universe for a business. Query FANMAP maps every angle for one specific query. These are different tools. Confusing them wastes hours.

**Change 2: The Orbit Map now runs between FANMAP and content writing.**
In v1.0, the Orbit Map was introduced as a competitor identification tool. In v2.0, it is the filter that sits between your raw FANMAP output and your content queue. It grades every topic by competitive environment, not just by hallucination probability.

**Change 3: FANMAP now has six types, not one.**
Different situations require different FANMAP configurations. A new client with no content needs a different approach than an established site retrofitting existing content. A business trying to dominate one funnel stage needs a different approach than one building full-funnel coverage.

---

### The v2.0 Research System in Full

The complete flow is linear. Each step feeds the next. No step is skippable without degrading output quality.

```
ICP Definition
     ↓
Base Keywords (5–8 broad terms from ICP)
     ↓
Business FANMAP (topic universe)
     ↓
Orbit Map (competitive environment grading on sampled queries + layers)
     ↓
O-grade and C-grade queries filtered
     ↓
Query FANMAP on each priority query (angle exploration + article decision)
     ↓
Competitor Study (USP gaps, language, intent, constraints, after-reading gaps)
     ↓
Content Plan — Phase 1 (O), Phase 2 (C), Phase 3 (S)
```

The critical insight about the Orbit Map position: it runs on a *sample* of your FANMAP output combined with layered queries. You do not run the Orbit Map on all 100 FANMAP topics. You run it on your base keywords plus layered variants. The result tells you which competitive environments your content will face, so you spend time on the right topics.

---

### What v2.0 Produces That v1.0 Did Not

A v1.0 FANMAP produced a topic list. A v2.0 FANMAP produces a content plan with:

- Topics prioritized by competitive environment grade, not just hallucination probability
- Each topic mapped to the ICP and funnel stage it belongs to
- An article decision for each topic: one article or multiple
- Phase assignments: Phase 1 (weeks 1–6), Phase 2 (weeks 7–16), Phase 3 (months 4+)
- Placement decisions: blog articles, landing pages, answer hubs, or FAQ sections

---

### Lesson Summary

- FANMAP v2.0 has two use cases: Business FANMAP (topic universe) and Query FANMAP (article planning)
- The Orbit Map runs between them as a filter, not at the end
- v2.0 has six FANMAP types for different situations
- Output is a phased content plan, not a topic list

## L2.2 — The Six FANMAP Types: When to Use Each

### Opening Hook

Every carpenter has different tools for different jobs. A hammer is not wrong when you need a screwdriver — it just produces a worse result.

FANMAP is a framework, but within it there are six configurations. Using the wrong one for your situation means either generating too much irrelevant data or missing the specific output you needed.

---

### Type 1: Standard Business FANMAP

**What it produces:** A complete topic universe for a business across all ICPs and all funnel stages. Minimum 100 queries. Typically 150 to 200.

**When to use it:** New client from scratch. No existing content strategy. You need the full landscape before deciding where to start.

**Configuration:** All three ICPs × all five funnel stages × base layers applied.

**Time required:** Two to three hours for full output.

**Common mistake:** Treating this as the final content plan. It is the raw material the Orbit Map filters. A Business FANMAP alone does not tell you which topics to prioritize — it tells you which topics exist.

---

### Type 2: Single-ICP Deep FANMAP

**What it produces:** An exhaustive map for one specific ICP across all funnel stages. Minimum 80 queries. Often 120 or more.

**When to use it:**
- One ICP drives 70%+ of revenue
- Limited budget — maximum depth in one direction
- Already ran Standard FANMAP and need to go deeper on primary ICP

**Configuration:** One ICP × all five funnel stages × minimum 15 queries per stage × all 12 layer types applied at least once.

**Key difference:** Depth over breadth. You build every possible content piece for one customer type.

---

### Type 3: Single-Stage FANMAP

**What it produces:** Comprehensive coverage of one funnel stage across all ICPs. Minimum 50 queries.

**When to use it:**
- Strong at awareness but losing buyers at consideration
- Need to dominate one funnel stage fast
- Existing site needs mid or bottom-funnel coverage

**Configuration:** All ICPs × one funnel stage × minimum 20 queries per ICP for that stage.

**Practical example:** A SaaS with strong blog content but nothing at the evaluation stage. Run Single-Stage FANMAP for Evaluation. Output: every comparison, alternative, and feature-validation query across all ICPs.

---

### Type 4: Query FANMAP (Single Topic Deep Dive)

**What it produces:** A complete article plan for one specific query. 8 to 12 sub-angles, article decision (one or multiple), titles, H2s, FAQs, word count.

**When to use it:**
- You have an O-grade query from the Orbit Map and need to plan the content
- You need to decide: one article or split into multiple
- A writer needs a brief

**The article decision rule:**
- Same ICP + same funnel stage + under 2,500 words → one article
- Different ICPs or funnel stages, or over 2,500 words to cover properly → multiple articles

**This is the most-used type day-to-day.** Every O-grade query gets a Query FANMAP before writing begins. No Query FANMAP means no brief means content that misses the fan-out.

---

### Type 5: Competitor Gap FANMAP

**What it produces:** Topics competitors own, and the angles within those topics they have NOT covered.

**When to use it:**
- Competitor consistently cited across your target queries
- Entering a market with established competitors
- Client displaced from citations they previously held

**The process:**
1. Identify what topics the competitor consistently covers
2. Run Standard Business FANMAP for the same business type
3. Cross-reference — every query in your FANMAP not in competitor content is a gap
4. Prioritize gaps where competitor's existing content still grades C or O

**Most valuable output:** After-reading gaps. Questions a reader still has after finishing the competitor's best article. The competitor creates the demand and fails to satisfy it. You provide the answer.

---

### Type 6: Seasonal FANMAP

**What it produces:** A time-based content calendar aligned to seasonal query patterns and buying cycles.

**When to use it:**
- E-commerce with seasonal demand (Eid, back-to-school, summer, winter)
- Event-based businesses (wedding season, conference season)
- Any business with predictable annual query spikes

**The publishing timing rule:** AI citation for a newly published article takes two to six weeks to establish. Seasonal content must publish six weeks minimum before the peak. Eight weeks is safer.

---

### Choosing the Right Type

| Situation | FANMAP Type |
|---|---|
| New client, no existing content | Standard Business |
| One ICP drives most revenue | Single-ICP Deep |
| Content gap at a specific funnel stage | Single-Stage |
| Ready to write a specific article | Query FANMAP |
| Competitor dominates your space | Competitor Gap |
| Seasonal or event-based business | Seasonal |
| Multiple products | One Standard FANMAP per product line |

---

### Lesson Summary

- Six types exist for six situations — wrong type wastes time
- Query FANMAP is the most-used type day-to-day
- Competitor Gap FANMAP targets after-reading gaps, not just missing topics
- Seasonal FANMAP must produce content six to eight weeks before the peak

## L2.3 — ICP Architecture: The Foundation Everything Else Is Built On

### Opening Hook

ICP stands for Ideal Customer Profile. Most people treat it as a demographics exercise — age, income, job title, location. Fill a template, move on.

That ICP is useless for AEO.

AEO content targets specific questions from specific people in specific situations. A demographic profile does not tell you which questions a 28-year-old in Lahore asks when their goldfish dies. The four-question model does.

---

### Part 1: The Four-Question Model

Every ICP for AEO is defined by four questions. These produce different information from demographic data.

**Question 1: Who?**
Not age and income. Situation and context. Where are they in life right now that makes them a potential buyer? What is their experience level with this product? What are their access constraints — budget, location, equipment, time?

The "who" in AEO is a situation, not a demographic segment.

**Question 2: What problem?**
The specific problem triggering the search. Not the general pain category — the specific thing that went wrong or the gap they are trying to fill. "Their goldfish died" is more useful than "they want a pet." "Support tickets take 48 hours and customers are complaining" is more useful than "they need better support."

The problem seeds the awareness-stage query. If you cannot state it in one specific sentence, the ICP is not defined well enough.

**Question 3: What triggered the search today?**
The problem has probably existed for a while. Something happened today that made them search right now. A child's birthday. A negative review from a customer. A summer heat wave that killed the fish. A competitor offering a feature they do not have.

The trigger determines urgency and tone. Someone whose fish just died searches with different language than someone researching before a purchase.

**Question 4: What does success look like?**
The specific outcome that closes the loop. Not general satisfaction — the concrete result. "Fish stays alive for at least a month." "Support response time under four hours." "Wedding runs on budget with no vendor surprises."

Success language appears in decision-stage queries and in review language from happy customers.

---

### Part 2: ICP Across Five Business Types

**Local Business**
- Who: Geographic proximity primary constraint. Experience level with product. Family situation.
- Problem: Cannot find good local stock. Got bad advice from a general store. Previous purchase failed.
- Trigger: Birthday, school project, home renovation, negative event with previous product.
- Success: Problem solved without drama. No need to return. Someone to contact if something goes wrong.

**E-commerce**
- Who: Budget is almost always a constraint. Buying without seeing or touching the product. Authenticity and delivery concerns.
- Problem: Cannot access locally. Price difference from local options. Quality or sizing uncertainty.
- Trigger: Saw online, friend recommendation, local store does not stock it, local price too high.
- Success: Arrives as described, correct specification, easy return if needed.

**SaaS**
- Who: Role and company size matter more than demographics. Decision-maker vs evaluator. Startup vs mid-market vs enterprise. Current tool stack.
- Problem: Current tool too expensive at scale, too complex for team size, missing a specific feature, or team is not adopting it.
- Trigger: Pricing changed. Team grew. Competitor mentioned a different tool. Current tool failed at a critical moment.
- Success: Team adopted without long onboarding. Specific metric improves. No IT involvement needed for implementation.

**Event Planning**
- Who: Budget and location are primary. Event planning experience level. Once-in-a-lifetime vs recurring.
- Problem: Overwhelmed by options. Previous vendor failed. Over budget from last event. Cannot find vendors who handle all aspects.
- Trigger: Fixed date set. Budget approved. Previous planner fell through. Family or boss pressuring for a decision.
- Success: Event runs as planned with no surprises. Stays on budget. Planner handles problems without client needing to intervene.

**Content / Education**
- Who: Career stage matters. Complete beginner, mid-level practitioner, or experienced professional upskilling. Budget for learning. Outcome needed — job, career change, client acquisition.
- Problem: Free content is scattered and inconsistent. Paid courses too expensive or too generic. Theoretical knowledge with no practical framework.
- Trigger: Job posting required a skill they do not have. Client asked for something they cannot deliver. Freelance income plateaued.
- Success: Can do the thing, not just understand it. Portfolio piece produced during course. Certification clients or employers recognize.

---

### Part 3: Multiple ICPs and How They Interact

Most businesses have more than one ICP. The mistake is treating each as a separate content strategy.

Sharing happens at the awareness stage. A beginner and an intermediate buyer both search "why is my goldfish not eating" — same trigger, different depth needed.

Divergence happens at consideration and decision stages. A beginner searches "best goldfish for beginners." An experienced buyer searches "fancy goldfish with low susceptibility to ich."

**The ICP mapping rule:** Write awareness content that works for all ICPs simultaneously. Write consideration, evaluation, and decision content specifically for each ICP. This is more efficient and produces better citation rates at both stages.

---

### Lesson Summary

- The four-question model (Who, Problem, Trigger, Success) produces better AEO briefs than demographic profiling
- ICP profiles differ across five business types but follow the same four questions
- Awareness content can serve multiple ICPs; consideration and decision content should be ICP-specific
- The trigger is the most underused element — it determines query urgency and language

## L2.4 — The Orbit Map in Full

### Opening Hook

The Orbit Map in v1.0 identified three competitor orbits. That was step one.

In v2.0, the Orbit Map does more. It grades the competitive environment for every query segment in your space. It tells you where content faces strong resistance, moderate resistance, and vacuum conditions.

Without it sitting between FANMAP and content writing, you are walking into battles without knowing what you are facing.

---

### Part 1: The Three Journeys

**Journey 1: Core Orbit (Deep Water)**
*Purpose:* Identify Real AI Competitors — brands consistently cited across multiple platforms on multiple queries.

Process:
1. Take your 5 to 8 base keywords
2. Run each in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google AI Overview
3. Note every brand cited
4. Flag brands appearing on 2+ platforms consistently

These are established, trusted competitors. Know who they are before planning — not to attack immediately but to route around them.

---

**Journey 2: Mid Orbit (Shallow Water)**
*Purpose:* Identify Active Competitors — growing presence, not yet dominant.

Process:
1. Apply all 12 layer types to base keywords
2. Run every layered variant in AI platforms
3. Note every brand NOT in Core Orbit that appears here

These are your most actionable targets. They have a strategy working in a limited scope. Study what they are doing right. Compete directly in their gaps.

---

**Journey 3: Outer Orbit (Empty Water)**
*Purpose:* Identify Lucky Competitors and hallucination zones.

Process:
1. In layered queries from Journey 2, look for where Core and Mid orbit competitors are absent
2. Note any unknown website or unrelated source cited
3. Flag queries with clear hallucination patterns

Lucky Competitors are cited from one viral piece with no ongoing strategy. Easily displaced.

Hallucination zones are where AI is genuinely struggling. O-grade content fills a vacuum here.

---

### Part 2: Building the Orbit Map Grid

Document as a grid before finalizing the content plan.

| Query | ChatGPT | Perplexity | Google AIO | Gemini | OCS Grade | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Base keyword] | [Source] | [Source] | [Source] | [Source] | S/C/O | |
| [Layer variant] | ... | ... | ... | ... | | |

**Row structure:** Base keywords plus every layered variant. Typically 30 to 50 rows.

**Grading logic:**
- Same high-authority sources across 3+ platforms → S
- Mixed sources, some gaps, some hallucination signals → C
- Weak answers, query deflection, random sources, or no sources → O

---

### Part 3: The Orbit Map and FANMAP Interaction

Your Business FANMAP produces topics. Your Orbit Map grades their competitive environment. The interaction:

1. Run Business FANMAP. Get full topic universe (100–200 queries).
2. Sample 10 to 15 representative queries plus base keywords.
3. Apply layers to the sampled queries.
4. Run Orbit Map on this sample. Grade every query OCS.
5. Use grades to filter the full FANMAP output.

**Why sample:** Running 200 queries across four platforms manually takes 12 to 15 hours. Sampling 30 to 50 representative queries takes 2 to 3 hours and produces 90% of the strategic insight.

---

### Part 4: Reading the Orbit Map Strategically

**Budget allocation:**

| Budget level | Orbit Map strategy |
|---|---|
| Limited | Focus exclusively on Outer Orbit. Fast wins. Lucky competitors easily displaced. |
| Medium | Target Mid Orbit. Requires better content than Active Competitors. 3–6 months. |
| Strong | Target Core Orbit. Sustained content, off-page authority, time. 6–18 months. |

**Always start in Outer Orbit regardless of budget.** Phase 1 O-grade citations build the domain trust that makes Phase 2 and Phase 3 more achievable.

**Platform weakness:** If Perplexity has weaker coverage in your topic space than Google AI Overview, Perplexity is your fastest path to visible early citations. Optimize for the weakest platform first.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Three journeys: Core Orbit (dominant), Mid Orbit (active), Outer Orbit (lucky + hallucination zones)
- Build the grid with query rows and platform columns, grade each OCS
- Orbit Map runs on a sample — not all 200 FANMAP queries
- Always start in Outer Orbit regardless of budget
- Platform weakness in your space = fastest path to early citation wins

## L2.5 — The Layering System Masterclass

### Opening Hook

Layers are what separate a surface-level FANMAP from a competitive intelligence tool.

"Goldfish care" has strong training data and multiple high-authority competitors. You are not winning there quickly.

"Goldfish care in Lahore apartment during load shedding in summer" has almost nothing in training data. The layering system is how you systematically find every combination like this across your entire topic space.

There are twelve layer types. Every O-grade opportunity you have missed so far was findable through a layer combination you did not try.

---

### The Twelve Layer Types

**Layer 1: Location**
Formula: [Topic] + [City/Area/Country]
Examples: goldfish shop DHA Lahore | running shoes Karachi | wedding venue Gulberg
Hallucination rate: ~72%
Best for: Local businesses, any query where local context changes the answer.

---

**Layer 2: Person Type**
Formula: [Topic] + [Who the person is]
Examples: goldfish for first-time owner | SEO for freelancer | wedding planning for couple abroad
Hallucination rate: ~52%
Best for: Any query where who is asking changes the right answer.

---

**Layer 3: Demographic**
Formula: [Topic] + [Age/Life stage/Family situation]
Examples: goldfish for kids under 10 | SEO for fresh graduates | running shoes for elderly men
Hallucination rate: ~65%
Best for: Consumer products with age-relevant considerations.

---

**Layer 4: Budget**
Formula: [Topic] + [Price constraint in local currency]
Examples: goldfish setup under 2000 PKR | SEO course under 5000 rupees | budget wedding venue Lahore
Hallucination rate: ~78%
Best for: Any purchasing decision. This layer almost always produces O-grade in local markets because AI does not have current local pricing data.

---

**Layer 5: Problem**
Formula: [Topic] + [Specific thing that went wrong]
Examples: goldfish not eating | website traffic dropped | wedding venue cancelled last minute
Hallucination rate: ~55%
Best for: Pre-awareness and awareness stage content. High-intent — the person is actively struggling right now.

---

**Layer 6: Time**
Formula: [Topic] + [Season/Urgency/Time period]
Examples: goldfish care Lahore summer | wedding planning 3 months before | SEO results how long
Hallucination rate: ~63%
Best for: Seasonal FANMAPs, urgency-driven content, topics where timing changes the answer.

---

**Layer 7: Comparison**
Formula: [Option A] vs [Option B] for [Context]
Examples: goldfish vs betta fish for beginners | Intercom vs Drift for small team | Udemy vs Coursera for Pakistan
Hallucination rate base level: ~30% | With location: ~60%
Best for: Consideration and evaluation stage. Very high intent — person is close to deciding.

---

**Layer 8: Negative**
Formula: Worst [Topic] for [Context] OR Why not [Topic] OR [Topic] mistakes
Examples: worst goldfish for beginners | why not buy cheap running shoes | SEO mistakes beginners make
Hallucination rate: ~60%
Best for: Awareness and consideration stage. Negative-layer queries attract readers who have already tried the obvious solutions and failed.

---

**Layer 9: Use Case**
Formula: [Topic] for [Specific scenario or purpose]
Examples: goldfish for office decor | running shoes for monsoon | SEO course for agency employees
Hallucination rate: ~70%
Best for: Any business with multiple distinct buyer use cases. Each use case is a separate citation opportunity.

---

**Layer 10: Outcome**
Formula: [Topic] that [specific result]
Examples: goldfish that survive Lahore heat | running shoes that reduce shin splints | SEO tactics that work in 2026
Hallucination rate: ~50%
Best for: Decision-stage content. The person is searching for confirmation a specific option produces the result they need.

---

**Layer 11: Qualifier**
Formula: Best/Easiest/Fastest/Cheapest/Most reliable [Topic] for [Context]
Examples: easiest goldfish to keep in Pakistan | fastest SEO results for new site | most reliable wedding venue Lahore
Hallucination rate base: ~48% | With location: higher
Best for: Consideration stage. Qualifier queries attract buyers comparing options.

---

**Layer 12: Equipment/Constraint**
Formula: [Topic] without [equipment] OR [Topic] with [specific constraint]
Examples: goldfish care without air conditioner | running without proper shoes | SEO without budget
Hallucination rate: ~80%
Best for: Any context where standard advice assumes equipment or resources the target customer does not have. This is almost always an O-grade layer.

---

### Layer Combinations and Hallucination Probability

| Layers applied | Approximate O-grade rate |
|---|---|
| Zero (base keyword) | ~15% |
| One layer | ~30% |
| Two layers | ~55% |
| Three or more layers | ~75% |

Each layer added narrows the audience but deepens the hallucination zone. Build a content plan with a mix:
- One-layer queries: broader reach, often C-grade, Phase 2 content
- Two-layer queries: good balance, often O/C, Phase 1–2 content
- Three-layer queries: narrow but fast citations, strong O-grade, Phase 1 content

Do not build a plan entirely of three-layer queries. You will own specific topics but have no broad authority. The Answer Hub model (Module 4) shows how to combine layers to build both specific citations and broad authority.

---

### Recommended Layer Combinations by Business Type

For local businesses: Location + Person Type, Location + Budget, Location + Problem

For e-commerce: Budget + Location, Comparison + Context, Use Case + Location

For SaaS: Company Size + Use Case, Comparison + Team Size, Problem + Integration

For education: Location + Budget, Outcome + Career Stage, Comparison + Context

---

### Lesson Summary

- Twelve layer types, each adding one dimension of specificity
- Equipment/Constraint and Budget (local currency) layers produce the highest hallucination rates
- Three-layer combinations produce O-grade queries ~75% of the time
- Mix one-, two-, and three-layer queries to balance broad authority with fast citation wins
- Use business-type-specific layer combinations when running Orbit Map Journey 2

## L2.6 — FANMAP Output to Content Queue

### Opening Hook

A FANMAP can produce 200 topics. A content team can publish two to four articles per month. That gap does not close with harder work. It closes with a prioritization framework that tells you exactly which articles to write in Phase 1, which to write in Phase 2, and which to hold for Phase 3.

---

### Part 1: The Three-Phase Structure

**Phase 1: O-grade topics (immediate)**
Fastest path to citations. Fill hallucination zones with no strong competitor. Citation timeline: two to six weeks on Perplexity, four to eight weeks on Google AI Overview.

Phase 1 contains five to eight articles. Enough to establish initial citation presence across multiple queries.

**Phase 2: C-grade topics (medium term)**
Requires better content than what currently exists. Real but not insurmountable competition. Citation timeline three to six months. Phase 1 authority building works in the background.

Phase 2 contains eight to twelve articles. These include consideration and evaluation stage content that converts the readers Phase 1 attracts.

**Phase 3: S-grade topics and pillar content**
Highly competitive broad queries. Approached after Phase 1 and Phase 2 have built domain trust. Timeline six months to over a year.

Phase 3 contains two to four pillar articles plus ongoing supporting content.

---

### Part 2: Prioritization Within Phase 1

Score each O-grade topic on three factors (1 to 5 scale):

**Factor 1: Information Gain**
How much original, specific, non-generic information do you have?
- Original data from your business: 5
- Constraint-specific knowledge nobody else has: 4–5
- General knowledge with a local angle: 2–3
- Generic knowledge: 1

**Factor 2: Business Relevance**
How directly does this topic connect to your core offering?
- Leads naturally to a conversion action: 5
- Builds authority but no conversion pathway: 2–3

**Factor 3: Hallucination Depth**
How weak is AI's current answer?
- Total topic change or hallucination: 5
- Vague hedged answer: 4
- Partial answer missing the constraint: 3
- Mostly correct with a small gap: 2

**Priority Score = Information Gain + Business Relevance + Hallucination Depth**

Maximum is 15. Topics scoring 12 to 15 are your first five articles. Topics scoring 9 to 11 are articles 6 through 10. Below 9, move to Phase 2 consideration.

---

### Part 3: Article Decision for Each Topic

**One article when:**
- All fan-out sub-queries belong to the same ICP and funnel stage
- Topic covered thoroughly in under 2,500 words
- All angles share the same reader intent

**Multiple articles when:**
- Fan-out sub-queries span different ICPs
- Angles require different depth for different audiences
- Topic has a parent query and distinct child queries that stand alone
- Any single angle requires more than 1,500 words to cover properly

A topic that becomes three articles is not a problem — it is three citation opportunities from one FANMAP entry.

---

### Part 4: Placement Decisions

| Content type | Placement |
|---|---|
| Broad educational, awareness stage | Blog / Answer Hub |
| Specific question, single ICP | Dedicated Answer Hub article |
| Comparison or VS content | Standalone comparison page |
| Brand vs brand | Alternative or VS landing page |
| Decision-stage, high intent | Service/product page FAQ or landing page |
| Local buyer guides | Blog with LocalBusiness schema |

---

### Part 5: The Content Calendar Rules

**One to two articles per week maximum.** More than two per week looks like a content farm. Regular, spaced publishing looks like a business consistently producing quality content.

**Publish supporting spokes within two weeks of the pillar.** Internal links cannot point to pages that do not exist.

**Phase 1 complete before Phase 2 begins.** All Phase 1 articles published, schema-validated, and given at least four weeks to accumulate signals before Phase 2 starts.

**Refresh dates built in from day one.** Every Phase 1 article gets a six-month refresh date added to the calendar at the moment of publishing.

---

### The Full FANMAP-to-Queue Workflow

```
Business FANMAP
      ↓
Sample + Layer → Orbit Map
      ↓
OCS grading across platforms
      ↓
Filter FANMAP output by grade
      ↓
Score O-grade topics (Info Gain + Business Relevance + Hallucination Depth)
      ↓
Top-scoring topics → Query FANMAP for each
      ↓
Article decisions (1 vs multiple) + Placement decisions
      ↓
Phase 1 queue (5–8 articles) + Publishing calendar with refresh dates
```

Total time from ICP definition to publishing calendar: three to four hours for a typical client.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Three-phase structure: O-grade first, C-grade second, S-grade third
- Prioritize within Phase 1 using a three-factor scoring system
- Article decisions come from Query FANMAP — one topic can produce multiple articles
- Placement decisions affect schema requirements and internal linking
- One to two articles per week, Phase 1 complete before Phase 2 starts, refresh dates built in from day one
