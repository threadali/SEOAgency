## L0.1 — What Changed Between v1.0 and 2.0

### The Honest Assessment of v1.0

v1.0 was a solid introduction. It covered what AEO is, how AI works at a surface level, the 3 pillars (consensus, information gain, semantic structure), E-E-A-T, FANMAP as a topic discovery tool, competitor types, AQCE writing, FAQ basics, off-page channels, and schema.

Students who applied it got their first citations. Local businesses appeared in Perplexity. A few blog articles showed up in Google AI Overviews. The framework worked.

But two gaps emerged consistently across students who had completed v1.0.

**Gap 1: They could get cited but not stay cited.**
Citations appeared on O-grade topics and then quietly disappeared three to four months later. The v1.0 course covered how to earn citations. It did not cover how to protect them, refresh them, or extend them into adjacent topics. Content decay happened without anyone noticing until AI referral traffic had already dropped.

**Gap 2: They could execute individual tasks but not run a system.**
A student could write an AQCE article. They could run a FANMAP. They could add schema. But the tasks did not connect into a workflow. Each article was treated as a separate project rather than a piece of a compounding architecture. The result: good individual pieces with no cumulative authority signal.

v2.0 fills both gaps. It goes deeper on every v1.0 topic and adds the system layer that connects them.

---

### The Three Structural Changes

**Change 1: The Orbit Map is now a filter, not an afterthought.**
In v1.0 the Orbit Map was introduced near the competitor section. Students used it to categorize competitors. In v2.0, the Orbit Map sits between FANMAP output and content planning. Every topic gets graded against competitive environment before a word is written. This single change eliminates the most common v1.0 mistake: spending six weeks writing content for S-grade topics.

**Change 2: Architecture before writing.**
In v1.0, content was planned article by article. In v2.0, the Answer Hub model is the unit of planning. A hub of eight interconnected articles produces topical authority signals that no individual article can produce alone. The brief system connects research output to writing input without gap. The publishing sequence turns isolated articles into a compounding system.

**Change 3: The refresh cycle is built in from day one.**
Every article published in v2.0 gets a six-month refresh date assigned at the moment of publication. Content decay is expected, scheduled for, and caught before citations are lost. v1.0 treated content as a campaign. v2.0 treats content as a maintained asset.

---

### Lesson Summary

- v1.0 produced first citations but left two gaps: citation retention and system-level execution
- Three structural changes in v2.0: Orbit Map as filter, architecture before writing, refresh cycle built in
- The course is designed as a system — individual modules are more powerful in sequence than in isolation

## L0.2 — From Getting Cited to Controlling Citations

### The Two Stages of AEO Maturity

**Stage 1: Getting cited**
This is where v1.0 left most students. A well-written O-grade article targets a hallucination gap. AI fills the gap with the article. Citation appears on Perplexity, then Google AI Overview. AI referral traffic trickles in. First proof of concept.

Stage 1 requires: a correctly identified O-grade query, an AQCE-structured article with genuine information gain, correct schema, and Bing indexing. When all four are in place, citation follows within two to eight weeks.

**Stage 2: Controlling citations**
This is where v2.0 takes students. Stage 2 is not about any one article getting cited. It is about the systematic position you hold in AI responses across your entire topic space.

Stage 2 requires: a topic cluster where you hold the authority signal, off-page consensus that corroborates your on-page claims, FAQ stacking that makes your definitions the ones AI uses, a monitoring system that catches changes before they become losses, and automation that makes the system sustainable without burning out.

The difference between Stage 1 and Stage 2 is not more work. It is different work. Stage 1 is content production. Stage 2 is authority architecture.

---

### The Compounding Authority Model

Stage 2 produces compounding returns in a specific way.

When AI cites you for one O-grade query, readers arrive. Some mention you in their content. Some leave reviews using your language. Some share your article in communities where AI retrieval systems find it. Each of these actions adds a new off-page signal. The next time AI processes a related query, your signal is incrementally stronger.

Over time, something specific happens. AI begins citing you for queries you never directly targeted. Adjacent topics within your cluster, related constraint combinations you did not write for, follow-up questions to your cited articles. This is the authority expansion phase of Stage 2 — when the system is producing value you did not explicitly build.

Businesses in Stage 2 authority expansion have a structural advantage that is very difficult to close. A competitor cannot simply publish better articles on your cited topics. They have to overcome your existing citation history, your off-page consensus layer, your FAQ stacking across ten articles, and your monitoring system that catches their entries before they can establish themselves.

This is what the course is actually building toward. Not individual citations — a position in your topic space that compounds.

---

### Lesson Summary

- Stage 1: getting cited (v1.0 destination). Stage 2: controlling citations (v2.0 destination)
- Stage 2 requires: topic cluster authority, off-page consensus, FAQ stacking, monitoring, automation
- The compounding authority model: each citation makes the next easier, leading to authority expansion on untargeted adjacent queries
- The competitive moat of Stage 2 is not replicated by single better articles — it requires time plus all layers simultaneously

## L0.3 — The 2.0 Practitioner Stack

### Opening Hook

A v1.0 practitioner has knowledge. They understand AEO principles and can execute individual tasks at a reasonable quality level.

A v2.0 practitioner has a stack. They have frameworks, tools, workflows, quality gates, and a measurement system that makes their work reproducible and scalable. The knowledge is the same. The system is what is different.

This lesson maps the full practitioner stack so you understand what you are building as you move through the course. Every module adds one layer to this stack.

---

### The Six-Layer Practitioner Stack

**Layer 1: AI Architecture Knowledge (Module 1)**
Understands RAG pipeline mechanics, fan-out query structure, confidence scoring, hallucination zone patterns, and platform-specific citation behavior differences. Uses this knowledge to make content decisions, not just to explain concepts.

*Practitioner test:* Can diagnose why a specific article is not being cited based on which stage of the retrieval pipeline it is failing at.

**Layer 2: Research System (Modules 2 and 3)**
Can run any of the six FANMAP types for any business type. Can build an Orbit Map from base keywords through three journeys. Can run the one-time competitor study and query-level analysis. Produces a prioritized, phased content queue from the combined output.

*Practitioner test:* Given a new client and a two-hour window, can produce a Phase 1 content queue of six to eight articles with OCS grades confirmed on at least three of them.

**Layer 3: Content Architecture (Module 4)**
Can build a complete Answer Hub from a FANMAP output. Writes briefs that contain all seven fields with genuine information from research. Makes correct publishing sequence decisions. Builds content decay schedules into the publishing calendar.

*Practitioner test:* Can map a complete Answer Hub — pillar, six spokes, FAQ hub page, internal linking map — for any topic in under two hours.

**Layer 4: Writing and FAQ System (Modules 5 and 6)**
Writes articles that pass all AQCE standards on first review. FAQ sections pass all eight criteria consistently. Human writing pass removes all ten killer phrases and structural patterns without prompting.

*Practitioner test:* Can take any existing article, run an AQCE audit against it, and produce a specific improvement plan that targets the correct failure point.

**Layer 5: Technical and Off-Page Authority (Modules 7 and 8)**
Can implement all four schema types correctly. Runs the four-step validation workflow. Can run a complete brand consistency audit. Manages review systems, Reddit warm-up, backlink strategy, and the AI mirror test monthly.

*Practitioner test:* Can take a new client website and produce a complete technical AEO audit with prioritized schema implementation plan in under four hours.

**Layer 6: Authority Engineering and Automation (Modules 9 and 10)**
Runs FAQ stacking across a topic cluster. Manages the monthly citation audit and AI SOV tracking. Can diagnose and recover from competitor displacement within a standard timeline. Has all five Claude Projects set up and running. Can deliver the standard AEO audit and monthly reporting format to a client.

*Practitioner test:* Can pitch AEO to a client who only knows SEO, demonstrate the gap live using their queries, and structure a retainer proposal with specific monthly deliverables and measurable KPIs.

### How to Use This Course

Each module builds on the previous one. The temptation is to jump to the modules that feel most immediately applicable — the writing modules for writers, the technical modules for developers. Resist this.

The system works because the layers are interdependent. An AQCE article written without a brief produced from research (Layer 3) will miss the constraints and customer language that make it specifically citable. A brief written without an Orbit Map grade (Layer 2) will target the wrong competitive environment. Schema implemented without the content meeting AQCE standards (Layer 4) labels a poorly extractable page as explicitly labeled for extraction — higher visibility, no improvement.

Read in order. Apply in order. After completing the full stack, you will revisit earlier modules and find depth you missed on the first pass — because you now understand what each layer feeds into.

---

- The v2.0 practitioner stack has six layers: AI architecture knowledge, research system, content architecture, writing and FAQ system, technical and off-page authority, authority engineering and automation
- Each layer has a practitioner test — a specific task you should be able to complete without the course notes
- Read and apply modules in order — the layers are interdependent, skipping creates gaps that appear later as unexplained failures
